# Section 1: The Shape of the Problem

## 1.1 EchoLeak: a vignette

On 11 June 2025, Microsoft published CVE-2025-32711 — assigned by Microsoft acting as CVE Numbering Authority — for a vulnerability in Microsoft 365 Copilot disclosed by the security firm Aim Labs (the research arm of Aim Security).[^echoleak-source]

[^echoleak-source]: The technical characterisation in this section relies primarily on Aim Labs' published writeup of EchoLeak. As with several agent-security disclosures from 2024–2025, the most detailed technical account originates with the disclosing security firm rather than with an independent third party. The paper treats this as adequate where, as here, the characterisation is corroborated by the CVE record, an established CNA's CVSS score, and acknowledgement by the affected vendor. Where vendor disclosures are uncorroborated by such secondary evidence, the paper treats them with greater caution. The published CVSS scores for CVE-2025-32711 are 9.3 Critical (Microsoft as CNA) and 7.5 High (NVD enrichment); the paper cites the Microsoft CNA score as canonical and notes the NVD's secondary scoring as a matter of source-discipline. What made the disclosure significant was not the severity score but the mechanism. The attack required no user click, no malicious attachment, no credential theft. It required only that an attacker send a single carefully crafted email to a target whose Microsoft 365 environment had Copilot enabled.

The mechanism was as follows. Copilot, in normal operation, indexes the contents of the user's mailbox so that it can answer questions about emails the user has received. When a user later asked Copilot a routine question — for instance, "summarise my recent meetings" — Copilot retrieved relevant content from the indexed mailbox to inform its response. The attacker's email, sitting in the indexed mailbox, contained instructions written in natural language and addressed not to the human recipient but to the Copilot model itself: instructions to retrieve sensitive data from the user's tenant, encode it in a reference-style markdown image link, and exfiltrate it through an auto-fetched image whose URL chained through a Microsoft Teams proxy permitted by Copilot's content security policy. Copilot, processing the email's contents as part of its retrieval-augmented response, executed those instructions. The user never saw the malicious email. The user never typed anything related to it. The user simply asked an unrelated question, and the attack ran.

EchoLeak is not the first attack of its kind, nor the most sophisticated. Other disclosures, both before and after EchoLeak, explored adjacent attack surfaces — Johann Rehberger's SpAIware (BSides Vancouver Island, 20 September 2024), the AgentFlayer chains presented by Zenity Labs at Black Hat USA on 6 August 2025, and Operant AI's Shadow Escape disclosed on 22 October 2025. But EchoLeak crystallised something the others had only implied: that the attack surface of an autonomous agent system is not the surface of any individual component within it. The mailbox indexer behaved as designed. The retrieval system behaved as designed. The model invocation behaved as designed. The egress channel was, in normal use, sanctioned. Each component, evaluated against the controls under which it had been certified, did what it was specified to do. The vulnerability lived in the composition.

A note on selection. EchoLeak's Microsoft 365 Copilot configuration sits at the boundary of the paper's strict agent definition (developed in §1.2) — it satisfies foundation-model reasoning and persistent state clearly, exhibits tool-like retrieval behaviour, but operates with a more constrained form of delegated authority than the paper's central case. The vignette is selected not as the most autonomous agent deployment but as a clear, well-documented illustration of the composition-vulnerability mechanism, which appears in more pronounced form in agent systems with broader delegated authority.

This is the shape of the problem the paper takes up. It is not a problem of insecure components. It is a problem of architectures whose security properties cannot be inferred from the security properties of their parts. It is a problem regulated financial services have begun to confront, as the autonomous agent systems they have begun to deploy in production exhibit precisely this character. And it is a problem the established disciplines of risk management — model risk, AI governance, cybersecurity — were not designed to address.

A note on what the paper does and does not claim. The paper's contribution is a synthesis methodology — an analytical structure that organises existing regulatory anchors, technical taxonomies, and assurance disciplines into a framework specific to autonomous agents in financial services. The methodology is preliminary: it has been developed against the public record of agent deployments, supervisory communications, and security disclosures, and applied to public reference deployments, but has not yet been validated through application to live regulated production environments. Where it requires extension or empirical validation in live deployments, the paper says so. The paper is offered as a contribution to a developing field, not as a definitive statement of it.

## 1.2 What is an agent

The term "AI agent" is used in industry, in academic computer science, and in regulatory consultation documents to refer to systems with materially different architectures and risk profiles. The paper adopts a specific operational definition, both for clarity within the paper and to delimit the scope of the assurance framework that Section 4 develops.

For the purposes of this paper, an *AI agent* is a system that combines four capabilities:

1. **Reasoning over a foundation model.** The system uses a large language model — typically a frontier model from a major provider, deployed via API — as its primary reasoning component. Decisions about which actions to take, in what order, and with what parameters are produced by the model rather than by a deterministic policy.

2. **Tool invocation.** The system is configured with access to one or more *tools* — external functions, APIs, services, or systems — that it can invoke during the course of its reasoning. The set of tools is typically declared at session initialisation, but the model decides which to invoke and when. In contemporary deployments, tools are increasingly exposed via the Model Context Protocol (MCP), an open specification originally published by Anthropic on 25 November 2024 and donated on 9 December 2025 to the Agentic AI Foundation (AAIF), a directed fund under the Linux Foundation co-founded by Anthropic, Block, and OpenAI, with platinum members including AWS, Bloomberg, Cloudflare, Google, and Microsoft.

3. **Capacity for delegated authority.** The system is designed to act on behalf of a human principal — a user, an operator, an organisation — under authorisation granted in advance and exercised at runtime without per-action confirmation. The authorisation may be scoped narrowly (read-only access to a specific document store) or broadly (write access to a transactional system); some deployments configure agents with no delegated authority at all, requiring explicit confirmation for every action. What distinguishes the object class is the design: the system is built for delegation, even where a particular deployment chooses not to use it. The risk profile of an agent deployment depends substantially on the authority it is configured with, and the paper treats configured authority as a primary input to the assurance methodology Sections 3 and 4 develop.

4. **Persistent state across reasoning steps.** The system maintains context — sometimes called *memory* — across multiple reasoning steps within a session, and in some configurations across sessions. Persistent state is what distinguishes agents from stateless function calls; it is also what gives agents their characteristic ability to compose tool invocations into multi-step workflows.

This operational definition is narrower than some industry usages of the term, and broader than others. It is narrower than usages that include any LLM-augmented application — a chatbot answering questions about a fixed knowledge base does not, in this definition, qualify as an agent because it lacks tool invocation and delegated authority. It is broader than usages that restrict the term to fully autonomous systems exhibiting open-ended planning — most production agent deployments today are scoped to specific workflows, not general-purpose autonomy, but the paper considers them agents because they exhibit all four capabilities listed above.

Three categories of system are explicitly excluded from the paper's scope. *Robotic process automation* (RPA) systems automate workflows by scripted manipulation of user interfaces; they exhibit none of the four capabilities and operate under fundamentally different control regimes. *Classical machine learning systems* — credit risk scorecards, fraud detection models, IRB internal models — produce predictions or scores but do not invoke tools or exercise delegated authority; they are governed under the model risk framework and remain so. *Conversational AI systems without tool access* — for example, customer-service chatbots restricted to information retrieval from approved corpora — exhibit reasoning over a foundation model but lack the other three capabilities; their risk profile is meaningfully different.

A fourth category warrants separate treatment because it is increasingly conflated with agents in industry usage: *developer-assistance and human-supervised AI tools* — code completion assistants, IDE-integrated chat tools, document review assistants where each model output is reviewed by a human before it produces an effect. These systems may use foundation models and may even invoke tools, but the synchronous human gate on every output materially changes their risk profile. The threats the paper analyses — particularly those that exploit the absence of human review at the moment of execution — operate differently or do not arise in human-supervised configurations. The paper's framework is not designed for these systems and does not claim to assure them, though many of its principles transfer with appropriate adaptation.

The distinction matters because the assurance gap the paper documents is specific to systems that exhibit all four capabilities together. A system with reasoning but no tools cannot be subjected to tool poisoning. A system with tools but no delegated authority cannot exhibit the confused-deputy patterns of Section 3. A system with delegated authority but no persistent state has a smaller attack surface for toxic-flow patterns that compose across reasoning steps. The four capabilities, in combination, constitute the object class for which existing assurance disciplines fall short.

## 1.3 Deployment in regulated financial services

The deployment of AI agents in regulated financial services has accelerated through 2024 and 2025, and supervisory authorities have begun to document the trajectory in increasingly specific terms.

The European Central Bank's Single Supervisory Mechanism, in its *Supervisory Priorities 2026–2028* published on 18 November 2025, identified ICT and operational resilience as Supervisory Priority 2, with explicit focus on banks' use of AI — particularly generative AI — and a stated intention to expand supervisory engagement from prudentially-relevant AI applications to generative AI more broadly through targeted horizontal workshops. BaFin, the German federal financial supervisor, published *Guidance on ICT Risks in the Use of AI at Financial Entities* on 18 December 2025, treating AI systems as a sub-case of network and information systems under DORA Article 3(2), and addressing AI strategy, asset inventory, ICT lifecycle integration, and technical countermeasures including adversarial-input handling. De Nederlandsche Bank published *AI bij verzekeraars: kansen en risico's* on 27 March 2025, articulating supervisory expectations for AI deployments in the Dutch insurance sector and reporting that 15 of 36 insurers in its 2024 study had reported AI use in fraud detection and claims-related operations. The Autorité de contrôle prudentiel et de résolution articulated, in a public address by First Deputy Governor Denis Beau in Lisbon on 27 October 2025 ("A compass to guide us toward intelligent AI supervision") and a December 2025 article in *Archives de philosophie du droit* Tome 66, an evaluative methodology for AI in finance built around four interdependent criteria — appropriate data management, performance, stability, and explainability — described as the cardinal points of a compass and explicitly addressing cases falling outside the AI Act's narrow high-risk scope. The European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority issued Opinion EIOPA-BoS-25-360 on 6 August 2025, *Opinion on AI Governance and Risk Management*, addressing competent authorities and setting out baseline supervisory expectations across six areas (fairness and ethics, data governance, documentation, transparency and explainability, human oversight, and accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity) under the Insurance Distribution Directive, the Solvency II Directive, DORA, and Commission Delegated Regulations 2015/35 and 2017/2358.

The pace and density of these supervisory communications is itself diagnostic. Supervisory authorities do not publish at this cadence on technologies they consider speculative; they publish at this cadence on technologies they consider deployed and material. The paper is offered as a contribution to that body of work, not as a substitute for it.

A note on what the public record does and does not establish. The claim that AI use in regulated financial services is operationally material and supervisorily attended is well-evidenced: the supervisory communications enumerated above presuppose a population of AI-reliant systems substantial enough to warrant the institutional response. The claim that *agent-specific* production deployments — systems exhibiting the four capabilities defined in §1.2 — are widespread is more difficult to establish from the public record alone. Regulated firms rarely disclose AI-deployment specifics; the line between AI use and agent deployment is not always cleanly drawn in disclosures that exist; and quantitative claims circulating in industry are dominated by vendor surveys with methodological limitations. The DNB *AI bij verzekeraars* statistic (15 of 36 insurers reporting AI use in fraud detection and claim-amount estimation) is real and useful but does not by itself establish autonomous agent deployment under the paper's definition. What can be observed in addition to the supervisory record is the surrounding infrastructure: a rapidly developing MCP server ecosystem with named financial-services use cases (more than 10,000 published MCP servers as of December 2025, per the Linux Foundation announcement of the AAIF, with public registries listing FS-specific servers across several institutions); public commitments from major banks — JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, ING among them — to agent-based productivity and operational tools spanning pilots, internal deployment, and customer-facing production; and the supervisory documents themselves, which presuppose a population of deployed systems sufficient to motivate the regulatory response.

The trajectory is consistent with earlier waves of financial-services technology adoption. Cloud computing became a supervisory priority approximately three to four years after deployment scale crossed a threshold; threat-led penetration testing — formalised as CBEST by the Bank of England in 2014 and extended to the European level as TIBER-EU by the ECB in May 2018 — followed an analogous trajectory. The current cadence of AI-specific supervisory communication suggests that agent deployments have reached, or are approaching, the analogous threshold.

The question the paper takes up is increasingly less whether regulated firms will deploy agentic systems, and more how they will evidence that such deployments are controlled.

## 1.4 Five architectural properties that disrupt existing controls

AI agents, as defined in §1.2, exhibit five architectural properties that systematically disrupt the assumptions on which existing risk-management disciplines are built. The properties are not novel in isolation; each has analogues in earlier classes of system. What is novel is their combination, and the consequence of that combination for assurance methodology.

**Non-determinism.** The reasoning component of an agent is a foundation model whose outputs are probabilistic. Given the same inputs, the agent may produce different outputs across invocations — different tool selections, different parameter values, different reasoning paths. The probability distribution can be tightened (by lowering temperature, constraining outputs, or fine-tuning the model) but not eliminated. This contrasts with the disciplines that have governed financial-services systems historically. Model risk management as a discipline — under SR 11-7's lineage, its 2026 successor SR 26-2, and the European descendants PRA SS1/23 and the ECB Guide to Internal Models — is constructed around models whose inputs, assumptions, outputs, and validation samples can be specified as objects of review at validation time. Cybersecurity controls presume systems whose execution paths are determined by code and configuration. Agent execution paths are determined by model inferences whose distributions cannot be exhaustively characterised. The implication for assurance is that point-in-time testing — running an agent through a set of representative scenarios and observing its outputs — produces statistical rather than absolute guarantees, and the statistical guarantees degrade as the input distribution drifts away from the test set.

**Delegated authority at scale.** Agents typically operate under broader authorisation than the individual users on whose behalf they act. A customer-service agent may have read access to records for every customer, even though it should only access records for the customer currently in session. A claims-processing agent may have authority to approve disbursements up to a stated threshold, even though specific claims involve specific principals with specific entitlements. The standard architectural response — "service account" credentials held by the agent — solves the operational problem of provisioning access but creates a structural problem of principal propagation. The agent's authority does not, in the general case, narrow to match the requesting principal's authority. This violates an assumption that cybersecurity controls have rested on for decades: that the principal performing an action and the authority under which the action is performed are co-extensive. The implication for assurance is that access-control reviews of agent deployments must analyse not only what the agent is authorised to do, but the gap between what it is authorised to do and what any individual requesting principal is authorised to ask for.

**Composite tool chains.** Agents invoke sequences of tools, with the output of one tool becoming the input to the next. The set of tools is declared in advance, but the *composition* — which tools are invoked in which order with which arguments — is determined by the model at runtime. Existing cybersecurity controls evaluate tools individually: this tool, used by this principal, with these arguments, is or is not permitted. They do not evaluate compositions. A toxic-flow attack, as analysed in Section 3, is precisely an attack that exploits a composition of individually permitted invocations. Static permission systems are blind to it; runtime monitoring systems may detect it after the fact but cannot prevent it without prior compositional analysis. The implication for assurance is that the composition of tool invocations becomes a first-class object of analysis, distinct from the analysis of any individual tool, and existing methodologies provide no template for performing that analysis.

**Runtime-constructed reasoning context.** The reasoning that determines an agent's behaviour is conditioned not on a fixed input vector but on a context that the system assembles dynamically at runtime: the user prompt, the declared tool definitions and their metadata, the outputs of tool invocations, retrieved documents, persistent memory, and the intermediate outputs of the model's own prior reasoning steps within the session. This context is the actual input to the model's decisions, but it is constructed during execution rather than specified in advance, and it exists only momentarily unless deliberately captured. The property is intrinsic to how agents work; it is not a deficiency that can be engineered away without losing the capabilities that make agents useful. Three consequences follow. First, an agent's behaviour cannot be reconstructed after the fact — for incident investigation, supervisory examination, or outcome analysis — unless the runtime context was captured at execution time, because no static description of the system suffices to explain why the model reached a particular decision. Second, several of the attack patterns that the paper analyses in Section 3 operate by manipulating the runtime context (poisoning tool metadata, injecting instructions through retrieved content, corrupting persistent memory), and detection of such manipulation requires visibility into what the model saw. Third, the AI Act's record-keeping obligation under Article 12 — which requires that high-risk AI systems "technically allow for the automatic recording of events (logs) over the lifetime of the system" with traceability "appropriate to the intended purpose of the system," and Article 26(6)'s six-month minimum retention obligation on deployers — maps, in the case of agents, onto a logging architecture qualitatively more granular and voluminous than what model risk management or operational risk management typically requires. The implication for assurance is that the architectural fact — reasoning over runtime-constructed context — drives a derived requirement: the capture of that context must be a design property of the agent, not an operational concern addressed after deployment, because what is not captured at runtime cannot be reconstructed at audit time.

**Semantic attack susceptibility.** The reasoning component of an agent processes natural language as instructions. This is the property that makes agents useful, and it is also the property that makes them susceptible to attacks that operate at the semantic layer rather than the protocol or transport layer. A cybersecurity perimeter can prevent a malicious payload from reaching a system. It cannot prevent a benign-looking document, lawfully delivered through an authorised channel, from containing instructions that the agent reads and acts on. The mailbox in EchoLeak was not breached. The email was delivered through normal email infrastructure. The attack succeeded because the agent treated the email's contents as instructions, and the controls that govern the email infrastructure had no way to know that the contents were instructions to the agent. The implication for assurance is that agent-specific threats cannot be addressed by extending existing perimeter or content-inspection controls, because the threats live inside the envelope of what the existing controls were designed to permit.

The five properties, in combination, constitute what the paper terms the *architectural challenge* of agent assurance. Each would, in isolation, require some adaptation of existing methodology; their combination requires methodology the existing disciplines, individually or collectively, do not provide.

The properties recur as the analytical spine of the remainder of the paper. Section 2 examines the three established risk disciplines against them and argues each addresses some properties rigorously while leaving others outside its frame. Section 3 develops a threat taxonomy organised around the attack patterns the properties enable. Section 4 articulates the design principles an assurance framework must adopt to address the properties in combination.

## Bridge to Section 2

Section 2 turns to the existing disciplines themselves. Each addresses part of the agent assurance problem rigorously and well, but none addresses the whole, and the gap between them is structural rather than incremental. The architectural properties identified in this section are precisely the properties on which existing disciplines fall short.
# Section 2: Framework Insufficiency

Three risk disciplines have shaped how regulated financial services govern computational systems: model risk management, AI governance, and cybersecurity. Each is the product of a specific institutional history, addresses a specific class of problems, and is supported by a specific body of regulation, standards, and supervisory practice. Each is mature; each is rigorous in its own domain; each does work that the assurance of AI agents in financial services depends on.

The argument of this section is not that any of these disciplines is failed or obsolete. It is that each is *necessary but insufficient* for the assurance of autonomous agents — necessary because the work each does cannot be discarded, insufficient because the architectural properties of agents identified in §1.4 lie outside the frame each discipline was constructed to address. The synthesis framework Section 4 develops draws on all three, augments where each falls short, and is intended as complement rather than replacement.

## 2.1 Model risk management

Model risk management as a discipline traces its current form to the Federal Reserve and OCC's *Supervisory Guidance on Model Risk Management* (SR 11-7), issued in April 2011 and superseded in the United States by SR 26-2, the *Revised Guidance on Model Risk Management* issued jointly by the Federal Reserve, OCC, and FDIC on 17 April 2026. The European comparators most relevant to this paper's audience remain in force: the Bank of England's *Model Risk Management Principles for Banks* (PRA SS1/23), published 17 May 2023 as part of PS6/23 and effective 17 May 2024, applying to UK-incorporated banks, building societies, and PRA-designated investment firms with internal model approval; and the European Central Bank's *Guide to Internal Models*, with its July 2025 revision incorporating an expanded machine-learning section. The European discipline derives its analytical structure from the same lineage as SR 11-7 and SR 26-2, with adaptations for the Capital Requirements Regulation regime and for machine-learning models specifically.

A point of analytical importance in SR 26-2's scope: the revised guidance explicitly excludes generative AI and agentic AI models from its scope, characterising them as "novel and rapidly evolving" and signalling that an institution's general risk management and governance practices should guide their oversight in the absence of specific MRM coverage. The European supervisory regime has not made the equivalent exclusion explicit, but the gap SR 26-2 acknowledges is the gap this paper's framework is intended to address: agent assurance is, in the current US supervisory framing, definitionally outside MRM, and the synthesis methodology proposed here is one candidate structure for the governance work SR 26-2 redirects to general risk management.

The intellectual achievement of model risk management is the three-pillar validation triad: conceptual soundness, ongoing monitoring, and outcomes analysis. *Conceptual soundness* requires the model's design and theoretical foundations to be documented, justified, and subject to qualitative review. *Ongoing monitoring* requires that the model's performance be tracked continuously against expected behaviour, with thresholds for action. *Outcomes analysis* requires that the model's outputs be compared to realised outcomes, with feedback into the model's evaluation. This triad is genuinely powerful: it has governed credit scoring, market-risk models, internal ratings-based approaches, and stress-testing frameworks across global banking for more than a decade, and it remains the basis on which supervisors examine the institutions they oversee.

For agent assurance, model risk management contributes three things the synthesis framework retains. It contributes the principle that systems with material decision-making authority must be subject to validation rather than testing alone. It contributes the lifecycle discipline — design, validation, deployment, monitoring, retirement — that the synthesis framework adopts as its operational backbone. And it contributes the supervisory legibility of validation outputs: a financial-services supervisor reading the outputs of a model-risk validation under either SR 11-7 or SR 26-2 knows, in advance, what the document is going to claim and how to interrogate it. That legibility is rare and valuable, and the synthesis framework borrows from it deliberately.

Where model risk management falls short is at the points of contact with the architectural properties of agents. The deeper observation is not that the discipline assumes deterministic systems — SR 11-7 explicitly addressed quantitative models with stochastic elements, and SR 26-2 carries that recognition forward — but that the discipline is constructed around models whose inputs, assumptions, outputs, validation samples, and monitoring thresholds can be specified as objects of review. Agents stretch that construction because the object of assurance is no longer a model but a model-mediated workflow: the foundation-model reasoning, the tool inventory, the runtime context construction, the credentials and policies under which actions are taken, the memory persisted across reasoning steps, and the downstream effects of the actions themselves. Each of these elements can in principle be examined; their composition into the workflow that determines the agent's effective behaviour is what the discipline was not constructed to validate.

The property the paper terms *non-determinism* illustrates one dimension of the gap. The MRM validation framework, under both SR 11-7 and SR 26-2, presumes that a model's behaviour can be characterised by its outputs on a representative input space; sampling-based validation produces statistical guarantees that degrade as inputs drift away from the validation set. For an agent whose effective inputs are constructed at runtime from sources the system itself chooses, the input space relevant to validation cannot be specified in advance, and the discipline's sampling-based methodology produces guarantees that hold under assumptions the runtime configuration may not satisfy.

The challenge is sharper for the property the paper terms *runtime-constructed reasoning context*. MRM examines models whose inputs are specified at validation time and whose decision boundary is, in principle, a function of those specified inputs. An agent's reasoning is conditioned on a context assembled at runtime from many sources: tool definitions retrieved at session initialisation, document contents retrieved during execution, prior-step model outputs, persistent memory, and the user's current request. None of these inputs is specified at validation time. The discipline has no analogue for validating a system whose inputs are constructed during execution from sources determined by the system's own choices.

The property of *composite tool chains* presents a different kind of gap. MRM analyses models as units; it does not analyse compositions of models, and it does not analyse the situations in which the output of one model becomes the input of another. Agent deployments routinely chain tool invocations whose composition determines the system's effective behaviour. Validation of any individual tool invocation, in the MRM sense, produces no information about the safety of a composition, and the discipline provides no template for compositional analysis.

Finally, *semantic attack susceptibility* sits entirely outside model risk management's frame. The discipline addresses model performance, model fairness, and model robustness in statistical senses; it does not address adversarial inputs constructed in natural language to manipulate a model's reasoning at runtime. The threat surface MRM assumes is the input space the model was designed for, not the input space an attacker can construct.

The diagnosis is not that model risk management is wrong about the systems it was designed for, but that those systems and agent deployments are not the same class of object. The discipline's load-bearing assumptions — reproducibility from specified inputs, decomposition into validatable units, threat surfaces bounded by intended use — do not survive the transition. MRM contributes its discipline; what it does not contribute is the methodology for the discipline's application to systems with these architectural properties.

## 2.2 AI governance

AI governance is younger than model risk management, has a more diffuse institutional history, and rests on standards and frameworks rather than supervisory letters. Its current shape is largely defined by ISO/IEC 42001:2023 (published December 2023), the first international management-system standard for artificial intelligence, and by the United States National Institute of Standards and Technology's *AI Risk Management Framework* (NIST AI RMF 1.0, published January 2023), extended for generative AI by the *Generative AI Profile* (NIST AI 600-1, published July 2024). The European Union AI Act establishes obligations under EU law that overlap with these frameworks but are not coextensive with them; for the purposes of this section, "AI governance" refers to the management-system and risk-framework discipline rather than to AI Act compliance specifically, which Section 6 examines on its own terms.

The intellectual achievement of AI governance is the systematic application of management-system thinking to AI deployments. ISO 42001 establishes that an organisation deploying AI should have an AI management system — a structured approach to leadership, planning, support, operation, performance evaluation, and improvement specifically for AI — analogous to the information-security management system established by ISO 27001. The Annex A control catalogue specifies controls across organisational context, leadership commitment, AI-system lifecycle planning, resource provision, operational governance, performance evaluation, and continual improvement. NIST AI RMF 1.0 organises the same problem space around four functions — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage — that emphasise lifecycle progression and risk mapping rather than control specification.

For agent assurance, AI governance contributes three things the synthesis framework retains. It contributes the architecture of organisational accountability — who within an institution is responsible for an AI system, how that responsibility is documented, how it is reviewed. It contributes the lifecycle discipline at the institutional level (as opposed to model risk management's lifecycle discipline at the model level): the recognition that an AI deployment passes through phases requiring different governance interventions, and that those phases need to be visible to the institution's leadership. And it contributes the language of risk mapping and measurement that lets AI risks be discussed alongside other enterprise risks rather than treated as a special category.

Where AI governance falls short is at the level of technical specification. The frameworks specify that risks should be identified, controls should be established, and outcomes should be measured. They do not specify, in any operational detail, *which* risks attach to *which* architectural properties of *which* AI systems, or *what* controls actually address them. ISO 42001's Annex A controls are deliberately abstract; the standard is intentionally technology-agnostic and lifecycle-stage-agnostic, designed to accommodate AI systems ranging from classical ML models to autonomous agents under a single organisational frame. NIST AI RMF is similarly principle-based: it specifies that risks be mapped, not how to map them.

This abstraction is the discipline's strength for the broad question "is the institution managing its AI systems responsibly?" and its weakness for the specific question "is this autonomous agent deployment safe to operate?" The first can be answered in AI-governance terms by demonstrating that an AI management system exists, the deployment was inventoried, risks were assessed, controls were established, and performance is monitored. The second requires technical specification of which risks, which controls, and which performance signals — and AI governance provides none.

The architectural properties of agents make the gap concrete. *Composite tool chains* are not addressed in ISO 42001's Annex A; the standard's controls for AI-system design and operation are framed in terms appropriate to monolithic systems. *Runtime-constructed reasoning context* is not a concept in NIST AI RMF; the framework's measurement function presumes that what is to be measured is identifiable in advance. *Semantic attack susceptibility* sits outside both frameworks' threat models; the security controls in ISO 42001 reference ISO 27001's information-security controls, which were designed for traffic-and-access threats rather than semantic-input threats. *Capacity for delegated authority* receives some treatment in both frameworks under the heading of human oversight, but the treatment is principle-level — that human oversight should be designed into AI systems — without specification of how to assess whether oversight is sufficient for a particular configured authority.

AI governance is not wrong; it operates at a level of abstraction one removed from where the assurance work actually happens. It tells the institution what it must do; the synthesis framework must tell it how. AI governance is necessary because without it the institutional accountability structure for agent deployments collapses, insufficient because the technical specification it does not provide is precisely what determines whether an agent is safe to deploy.

## 2.3 Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is the oldest of the three disciplines, with the deepest institutional history, the broadest standards corpus, and the most specific supervisory presence in financial services. ISO/IEC 27001 in its 2022 revision (published October 2022) is the dominant international standard for information-security management systems. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework, in its 2.0 revision published 26 February 2024, provides the dominant non-prescriptive framework for organisational cybersecurity. In European financial services specifically, the Digital Operational Resilience Act establishes legally binding ICT risk management, third-party risk management, incident response, and resilience-testing obligations; DORA has applied to in-scope entities since 17 January 2025 and is supplemented by detailed Regulatory Technical Standards specifying implementation, including the RTS on ICT risk management framework (Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2024/1774) and the RTS on threat-led penetration testing (Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2025/1190).

The intellectual achievement of cybersecurity is the institutional treatment of adversarial threat. Where model risk management presumes the threats to a model are intrinsic (error, drift, miscalibration) and AI governance presumes the threats are systemic (poor governance, inadequate accountability), cybersecurity presumes the threats are adversarial — intelligent attackers attempting to compromise the system. This presumption shapes the discipline's controls, testing methodologies, incident-response architectures, and supervisory expectations. Threat-led penetration testing — formalised as CBEST by the Bank of England in 2014, extended to the European level as TIBER-EU by the ECB in May 2018, and incorporated into DORA's Articles 25–27 — is the methodology by which institutions test controls against intelligent adversaries rather than against random failure.

For agent assurance, cybersecurity contributes more than either of the other two disciplines. It contributes the threat-led testing methodology the synthesis framework adopts directly; the architecture of identity, perimeter, and access management on which agent deployments depend; the incident-response and forensics discipline without which agent deployments cannot be operated safely; and the regulatory infrastructure — DORA, its RTS, the supervisory expectations — that frames ICT risk management for regulated financial-services institutions. The synthesis framework Section 4 develops is not a replacement for cybersecurity; it is, in significant part, an extension of cybersecurity's methodology to a class of systems for which existing cybersecurity controls are insufficient.

Where cybersecurity falls short is at a precise boundary the discipline did not need to address before agents existed. Modern cybersecurity is sophisticated at identity management, access control, network segmentation, endpoint protection, third-party risk management, and incident response; the discipline's controls do real and necessary work across each of these domains. What it lacks is a native method for two adjacent problems: semantic instruction-following inside authorised workflows, where content the agent is authorised to read can manipulate the model's reasoning without violating any access control; and compositions of individually permitted tool calls, where each call is sanctioned but the composition produces an unsanctioned outcome. The architectural property of *semantic attack susceptibility* names the first; the lethal trifecta and toxic flows examined in §3.2 name patterns that exploit the second. The EchoLeak vignette in §1.1 illustrated an attack in which no perimeter was breached, no identity was compromised, no protocol was violated, and the attack nonetheless succeeded — because the attack operated on the semantic interpretation of content the agent was authorised to read, and produced its outcome through a composition of authorisations each of which was individually correct.

The property of *composite tool chains* presents a similar gap. Cybersecurity controls operate per-action: this principal, performing this action, with these arguments, is or is not permitted. The disciplines does not analyse compositions of permitted actions to determine whether the composition is itself permitted. Toxic-flow attacks, which Section 3 analyses in detail, exploit precisely this gap: each individual tool invocation is authorised under the deployment's access controls; the composition is not analysed and not detected.

The property of *capacity for delegated authority* exposes the deepest cybersecurity gap. The discipline's foundational assumption — visible in NIST SP 800-207 on Zero Trust Architecture (published August 2020), in OAuth 2.1's design, in DORA's identity-and-access management requirements — is that the principal performing an action and the authority under which the action is performed are co-extensive. An agent operating with service-account credentials on behalf of many users at once breaks this assumption at the architectural level. The agent is the principal of record from the cybersecurity layer's perspective; the principal who actually requested the action is invisible to the layer's controls. The MCP specification's 18 June 2025 revision addressed part of this gap by introducing the OAuth 2.0 Resource Server pattern (RFC 9728 Protected Resource Metadata) with mandatory Resource Indicators (RFC 8707) to bind tokens to specific MCP servers, but the deeper problem of principal propagation through tool chains remains an architectural concern the protocol does not solve.

The property of *non-determinism* sits at the edge of cybersecurity's frame in a different way. Cybersecurity controls do not require deterministic execution; they require auditable execution. An agent's non-determinism does not, by itself, violate cybersecurity controls. But the *property of runtime-constructed reasoning context* compounds with non-determinism to produce a configuration in which an agent's actions cannot be reconstructed without runtime context capture, and the absence of that capture is invisible to cybersecurity controls that operate on traffic and access events rather than on model reasoning context. Agent deployments routinely satisfy traditional cybersecurity logging requirements while remaining incapable of supporting incident reconstruction at the level the disciplines's own incident-response methodology presumes.

Cybersecurity is not wrong. Its controls do the work they were designed for, and the synthesis framework presumes them as foundation. But the threat models the discipline addresses — protocol exploitation, perimeter breach, identity compromise, traffic interception — were constructed for a class of system in which the model's reasoning was not part of the threat surface. Agent deployments make model reasoning a primary threat surface, and cybersecurity, considered alone, has no native methodology for that surface.

## 2.4 The structural shape of the gap

Taken together, the three disciplines address a substantial portion of the assurance work agent deployments require: validation discipline for systems with material decision-making authority (MRM); the institutional architecture of accountability and lifecycle (AI governance); the adversarial testing methodology and the regulatory infrastructure of operational resilience (cybersecurity). The synthesis framework in Section 4 is built on these contributions, not in replacement of them.

What the three disciplines do not, individually or collectively, address is the assurance of the architectural properties identified in §1.4. Each discipline addresses some properties at the level of principle but none at the level of operational specification. The properties most central to agent risk — composite tool chains, runtime-constructed reasoning context, semantic attack susceptibility — sit in the gap between the disciplines rather than within any of them.

The structural shape of the gap, then, is not a deficit in any single discipline. It is the absence of a discipline that addresses the architectural properties of agent deployments in their own terms, drawing on the three established disciplines without being subsumed by any of them. Section 3 of the paper develops a threat taxonomy that operates at the level of those properties. Section 4 develops a synthesis framework whose design principles — evidence-first, framework-anchored, regulator-legible, composable and continuous, and principal-bound — are constructed precisely to address the gap this section has identified.
# Section 3: Threat Taxonomy

The threat taxonomy this section develops serves three distinct purposes within the paper's argument. It documents, for an audience of senior risk and compliance leaders, the empirical state of agent-specific threats as of early 2026: which attacks have been demonstrated, by whom, against what systems, and with what consequences. It provides, for the supervisory and standards communities, a vocabulary that aligns with the consensus taxonomy emerging through the OWASP GenAI Security Project rather than introducing parallel terminology. And it furnishes the synthesis framework Section 4 develops with concrete threat referents — the patterns its design principles are constructed to address. The section presents the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications (2026 edition) as the structural spine, examines five cross-cutting attack patterns in depth, and closes with the amplifications specific to regulated financial services.

## 3.1 The OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications as structural spine

The OWASP GenAI Security Project published the *OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications* (2026 edition) on 9 December 2025, launching it at the London Agentic Security Summit during Black Hat Europe 2025, distilling the earlier *OWASP Agentic AI Threats and Mitigations v1.1* (February 2025) into a ten-category taxonomy.[^owasp-attribution] The taxonomy is the most widely-referenced consensus articulation of agent-specific threats as of the paper's writing, and the paper adopts it as the structural reference point against which more granular analysis is conducted.

[^owasp-attribution]: The Top 10 for Agentic Applications is itself the consensus output of an open working group within the OWASP GenAI Security Project; the paper treats the categories and their public examples as the published OWASP positions rather than as the contributions of any individual researcher. Where this section's analysis goes beyond what OWASP publishes — for instance, in the discussion of cross-cutting patterns in §3.2 — the analysis is the paper's, not OWASP's.

The ten categories, in OWASP's published numbering and titles, are:

| ID | Category | Public example cited by OWASP |
|---|---|---|
| ASI01 | Agent Goal Hijack | EchoLeak |
| ASI02 | Tool Misuse | Amazon Q |
| ASI03 | Identity & Privilege Abuse | — |
| ASI04 | Agentic Supply Chain Vulnerabilities | GitHub MCP exploit |
| ASI05 | Unexpected Code Execution | AutoGPT RCE |
| ASI06 | Memory & Context Poisoning | Gemini Memory Attack |
| ASI07 | Insecure Inter-Agent Communication | — |
| ASI08 | Cascading Failures | — |
| ASI09 | Human-Agent Trust Exploitation | — |
| ASI10 | Rogue Agents | Replit meltdown |

The taxonomy's analytical strength is its grounding in observable attack surfaces. Each category corresponds to a class of vulnerability demonstrated in production agent deployments, and the categories are framed in terms — goal hijack, tool misuse, supply chain, memory poisoning — that map onto established security vocabularies while introducing the agent-specific dimensions where existing vocabularies fall short. The taxonomy is also explicitly compositional: real attacks frequently span multiple categories, and the categories should be treated as a vocabulary for analysis rather than as mutually exclusive partitions of the threat space.

The compositional character of the taxonomy is critical for the analysis that follows. The patterns this section examines in depth — tool poisoning, identity and privilege abuse, the lethal trifecta, toxic flows, and zero-click exfiltration — are not synonyms for individual ASI categories. Tool poisoning exploits ASI04 and ASI02 in combination. Identity and privilege abuse maps to ASI03 but draws on ASI06 in some configurations. The lethal trifecta is by definition compositional: the term names the unsafe combination of capabilities (sensitive data access, untrusted input, egress channel) that produces ASI01 outcomes. Toxic flows compose ASI02 and ASI04 across tool sequences. Zero-click exfiltration compounds ASI01 and ASI09. The patterns are compositions because real agent attacks are compositions, and the OWASP categories are designed to support compositional analysis rather than to substitute for it.

The relationship between OWASP categories as ground-truth vocabulary and cross-cutting patterns as analytical lenses lets the paper avoid two failure modes the threat-taxonomy literature commonly exhibits: the proliferation of bespoke taxonomies that fragment the field (the paper introduces no competing taxonomy and treats OWASP's categories as canonical for the technical community's shared vocabulary), and the conflation of categories with patterns (the patterns in §3.2 are explicitly multi-category, and the analysis is explicit about which OWASP categories each composes).

A note on evidence grading. The threat taxonomy that follows draws on incidents, benchmarks, and research outputs of materially different evidentiary character. To be transparent about which sources support which weight of claim, the paper applies a four-tier evidence-grading convention:

- **Grade A.** Sources with regulatory, official-supervisory, or independent peer-reviewed status: regulations and supervisory publications, CVE records published in NVD, peer-reviewed academic papers and benchmarks. Adequate to support load-bearing claims.
- **Grade B.** Research-laboratory disclosures with independent vendor acknowledgement or with corroboration from a second independent technical source. Adequate to support incident analysis with appropriate caveat.
- **Grade C.** Vendor blog posts, conference demonstrations, or synthetic proof-of-concept disclosures without independent corroboration. Useful as indicative evidence but not as sole support for substantive claims.
- **Grade D.** Market commentary, press speculation, or assertions without identifiable empirical basis. Used only as background.

The grade is applied at first citation of each incident or benchmark; subsequent references in the section do not repeat the tag. A consolidated source-status table appears in the appendices. The convention's purpose is not to discount vendor disclosures categorically — several of the most informative agent-security disclosures of 2024 and 2025 originated from research firms — but to make the evidentiary basis of each claim visible to the reader.

## 3.2 Five cross-cutting attack patterns

The five patterns examined below were selected against three criteria: they have been demonstrated in production through public disclosures whose technical content is sufficient to enable analysis; they cut across multiple OWASP categories and reveal compositional dynamics that single-category analysis cannot; and they exercise the architectural properties identified in §1.4 in ways the existing risk disciplines examined in Section 2 do not natively address. The patterns are not exhaustive of the agent threat landscape; they are the patterns that most directly motivate the synthesis framework Section 4 develops.

### 3.2.1 Tool poisoning

*Tool poisoning* describes attacks in which malicious instructions are embedded in the metadata that tools publish to agents — tool descriptions, parameter schemas, type hints, default values, and tool output formats — rather than in user-provided input. The agent reads tool metadata as part of its reasoning process, and treats embedded instructions as if they were part of its own context. The attack composes ASI04 (Agentic Supply Chain Vulnerabilities) with ASI02 (Tool Misuse): the supply-chain dimension reflects that the attacker controls a tool the agent has been authorised to use, and the tool-misuse dimension reflects that the agent acts on the tool's instructions in ways that exceed the tool's documented purpose.

The pattern is distinct from indirect prompt injection through tool *output*. Tool metadata is registered, declared, and approved at the install or session-initialisation stage; an agent's MCP client reads it once per session as a precondition of being able to invoke the tool. Tool output, by contrast, is generated at runtime in response to specific tool calls, and reflects the tool's behaviour against specific inputs. Both surfaces can carry instructions that manipulate the model's reasoning, but the lifecycle and defensive posture differ: metadata can be hashed and pinned at registration; output cannot be pre-verified. The paper treats tool poisoning narrowly, as the metadata-layer pattern; attacks that exploit tool output content are analysed under §3.2.3 (lethal trifecta) where the composition of authorised reads with privileged access and egress produces the exploit.

The architectural property the pattern exploits is *runtime-constructed reasoning context*. Tool metadata is a component of the runtime-constructed context — the agent reads tool descriptions to decide which tool to invoke, and reads parameter schemas to determine how to invoke it. Each of these reads is a vector for instructions that the model treats as part of its reasoning. The property is intrinsic to how agents work: an agent that does not read tool metadata cannot decide which tool to use, and metadata that is not inspected cannot be poisoned but also cannot guide reasoning.

The MCPTox benchmark (Wang et al.), first released as an arXiv preprint in August 2025 (arXiv:2508.14925) and subsequently appearing in AAAI 2026 proceedings (*Grade A*: academic benchmark, peer-reviewed in proceedings publication), provides the canonical demonstration. The benchmark constructs adversarial MCP servers whose tool metadata embeds instructions intended to manipulate connected agents, and tests systematic susceptibility across major foundation models. The findings establish that tool poisoning is not an artifact of any single model's behaviour but a category-level vulnerability arising from how agents process tool metadata as input to reasoning. The benchmark differentiates among the metadata sub-vectors: *description poisoning* embeds instructions in human-readable tool descriptions; *schema poisoning* embeds them in parameter names, type hints, or default values; *output poisoning* places instructions in tool return values that the agent reads back into its context. *Rug pulls*, in which a tool ships benign metadata at install time and changes it after the deployer has approved it, exploit the fact that MCP clients re-read metadata at session initialisation by default; the agent has no native mechanism for detecting that the metadata has changed since approval.

Traditional cybersecurity controls do not address tool poisoning because they were not designed to evaluate semantic content for instruction-like patterns. Input sanitisation operates on user-provided content; tool metadata is not user-provided. Code review examines code that the agent executes; tool metadata is not code. Access control determines which principals can invoke which tools; the metadata of an invoked tool is read as a precondition of invocation, not as part of it. Model risk management has no analogue of metadata-as-input because the discipline assumes inputs are specified at validation time. AI governance frameworks specify adversarial-input testing without specifying that tool metadata is one such input. The gap is the absence of a discipline that examines the metadata layer specifically.

### 3.2.2 Identity and privilege abuse

*Identity and privilege abuse* describes attacks in which an agent exercises authority that is technically within its permitted scope but broader than the intent of the request that triggered it. The pattern is the agent-specific incarnation of the classical *confused deputy* problem: the agent holds tokens authorising a set of actions, a requester asks the agent to perform an action, and the agent uses its authorisation to perform an action that the requester never had authority to ask for. The pattern maps to ASI03 (Identity & Privilege Abuse) directly, and exploits the architectural property the paper terms *capacity for delegated authority*.

The architectural fact the pattern exploits is that the agent's authority and the requesting principal's authority are not, in standard agent deployments, co-extensive. A customer-service agent typically holds credentials that allow it to read records for any customer; a specific user request comes from a specific customer with specific entitlements. The agent's design permits the customer to ask the agent for their own records; the agent's authorisation permits the agent to retrieve any customer's records. The gap between the customer's entitlement and the agent's authorisation is the surface on which identity-and-privilege-abuse attacks operate.

The General Analysis disclosure of 6 July 2025 (*Grade B*: research-laboratory disclosure with vendor acknowledgement) provides a documented case.[^general-analysis-source] A Cursor agent connected to a Supabase database via MCP held `service_role` credentials — credentials with broad database privileges, designed for the agent's own use rather than for any individual user. A support ticket containing crafted instructions caused the agent to retrieve `integration_tokens` from the database and to write them into the ticket thread. The database's access controls did not prevent the action because the service-role credentials authorised it. The ticket-system's access controls did not prevent it because writing to a ticket thread is a sanctioned action for an authorised user. The attack succeeded because the deployment's authority architecture did not narrow the agent's authorisation to match the requesting principal's authorisation.

[^general-analysis-source]: The General Analysis disclosure was conducted on synthetic data in a controlled test environment; the technical mechanism is corroborated by independent security writeups (notably Simon Willison's blog post analysing the disclosure) and by Supabase's published security response confirming the disclosure timeline.

The architectural depth of the pattern can be illustrated by considering what would be required to prevent it. Narrowing the agent's authority to match each request would require *principal propagation*: the requesting user's identity flowing through the agent's reasoning into the database query, with the database enforcing row-level access against that propagated identity. The MCP specification's 18 June 2025 revision introduced the OAuth 2.0 Resource Server pattern (RFC 9728 Protected Resource Metadata) with mandatory Resource Indicators (RFC 8707) binding tokens to specific MCP servers, addressing the related but narrower concern of token forwarding. The deeper problem of principal propagation through tool chains — making the requesting user's identity, not the agent's, the principal of record at the database — remains an architectural concern that the protocol does not solve. Just-in-time credential issuance, scope narrowing per request, and explicit principal propagation are all controls that exist; none is native to MCP, and none is implemented by default in production deployments.

The Asana incident of June 2025 — discovered 4 June 2025 with the MCP server taken offline 5 June and restored 17 June 2025, affecting approximately 1,000 customers — presents a related but distinct failure: a tenant-isolation logic flaw in Asana's MCP integration exposed approximately one thousand customers' data to other MCP users. The mechanism was not prompt injection but classical access-control failure at the tenant boundary, and the disclosure is included here not as an example of identity and privilege abuse in the sense above but as a reminder that the agent layer can also fail in ways that resemble traditional access-control vulnerabilities. The synthesis framework must address both the agent-specific and the classical failures, because both occur in production deployments.

Cybersecurity addresses the classical access-control component of identity and privilege abuse rigorously through identity-and-access-management practice. What it does not address natively is the gap between agent authority and requesting-principal authority — because the discipline's foundational assumption is that the principal performing an action is the authority under which the action is performed, and that assumption breaks when the principal is an agent acting on behalf of many users. Model risk management does not address the pattern; AI governance frameworks specify that human oversight should be designed in but do not specify how to architect principal propagation. The gap is structural.

### 3.2.3 The lethal trifecta

*The lethal trifecta* is a compositional pattern named by Simon Willison in his blog post of 16 June 2025 ("The lethal trifecta for AI agents: private data, untrusted content, and external communication") for the configuration in which an agent simultaneously has access to sensitive data, exposure to untrusted input, and an egress channel through which information can leave the system's perimeter. The framing captures something the OWASP categories illuminate but do not isolate: each of the three capabilities is, individually, defensible and present in many legitimate agent configurations; the combination produces a class of attack against which individual-capability controls are systematically insufficient. The pattern composes ASI01 (Agent Goal Hijack), ASI02 (Tool Misuse), and ASI03 (Identity & Privilege Abuse), and exploits the architectural property *semantic attack susceptibility*.

The mechanism is straightforward to articulate and difficult to control. An attacker places content the agent will read — an email in a monitored inbox, a document in an indexed corpus, an issue in a tracked repository — that contains instructions written in natural language addressed to the model. The instructions direct the agent to retrieve sensitive data from sources the agent is authorised to access, and to egress that data through a channel the agent is authorised to use. Each step in the chain is, considered individually, a sanctioned action: the agent is authorised to read the inbox, authorised to access the data store, authorised to send email or create pull requests or write to ticket threads. The composition of sanctioned actions produces an unsanctioned outcome.

Three demonstrations illustrate the pattern's generality across deployment configurations. The Aim Labs EchoLeak disclosure (CVE-2025-32711, published 11 June 2025) (*Grade A*: CVE record, vendor advisory, multiple independent technical writeups), described in §1.1, is a lethal trifecta in Microsoft 365 Copilot: the indexed mailbox provides untrusted input, the tenant data provides sensitive data, the URL-based exfiltration channel via reference-style markdown auto-fetched images chained through a Microsoft Teams proxy provides egress. The Zenity Labs *AgentFlayer* presentation at Black Hat USA on 6 August 2025 by Michael Bargury and Tamir Ishay Sharbat (*Grade B*: research-laboratory disclosure with multi-platform corroboration) demonstrated the pattern across multiple agent platforms — ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot Studio, Salesforce Einstein, Google Gemini, Microsoft 365 Copilot, and Cursor with Jira MCP — establishing that the trifecta is a configuration-level vulnerability rather than a flaw in any single platform's implementation. The platforms were not all identical in mechanism; the pattern was.

The Invariant Labs GitHub MCP disclosure of 26 May 2025 (*Grade B*: research-laboratory disclosure with vendor acknowledgement)[^invariant-source] illustrates the trifecta in a developer-tool deployment configuration. An agent integrated with the official GitHub MCP server received a malicious GitHub issue body that contained instructions, written in natural language and addressed not to the human user but to the model, to retrieve private repository contents and exfiltrate them via a public pull request. The mechanism composes the three trifecta elements precisely: the issue body provides untrusted input through a tool channel the agent was authorised to read; the private repository provides sensitive data that the agent's credentials authorised it to access; the public pull request provides an egress channel that the agent was authorised to create. No code in the GitHub MCP server was vulnerable. No protocol was violated. No credential was compromised. The vulnerability lived in the composition of three sanctioned authorisations.

[^invariant-source]: The Invariant Labs disclosure is authored by the disclosing security firm. The technical characterisation is corroborated by independent security reporting (notably Simon Willison's blog post analysing the case as a canonical lethal-trifecta instance) and by GitHub's published incident response confirming the disclosure timeline and the affected configuration.

The cybersecurity perspective on the trifecta is instructive. Data Loss Prevention systems are designed to inspect outbound traffic for sensitive content patterns and block exfiltration. Against the trifecta, DLP either does not apply — because the egress channel (a sanctioned email, PR, or ticket thread) is one DLP is configured to permit — or applies too broadly, blocking the sanctioned channel and breaking legitimate use. The trifecta operates inside the envelope of what DLP was designed to permit, because each individual action it composes is one DLP correctly judges as legitimate.

The pattern's broader implication is that compositional analysis is a first-class assurance task the existing disciplines do not perform. Cybersecurity controls evaluate per-action; the trifecta is a property of compositions of actions. AI governance specifies adversarial testing without specifying compositional testing. Model risk management does not address compositions because it analyses models as units. The synthesis framework's *composable and continuous* principle, articulated in Section 4, is the direct response: the assurance methodology must reason about composition at the architectural level, before any individual action is taken.

### 3.2.4 Toxic flows

*Toxic flows* describes the static analysis of agent tool chains to identify compositions of individually-permitted invocations that, taken together, produce exploitable workflows. The terminology originates in the productisation of compositional analysis by Snyk Agent Scan, which following Snyk's 24 June 2025 acquisition of Invariant Labs[^snyk-invariant] introduced toxic-flow detection as the issue class TF001 (currently shipped in version 0.3.0 of the Agent Scan tool, accessible via the `--full-toxic-flows` flag). The pattern composes ASI02 and ASI04 across tool sequences and exploits both *composite tool chains* and *runtime-constructed reasoning context* as architectural properties.

[^snyk-invariant]: The vendor origin of the term "toxic flows" should not be read as a vendor-specific phenomenon. Compositional analysis of authorised actions has long-standing precedent in classical security analysis (notably in static taint analysis for traditional applications), and the agent-specific application of the analytical pattern is supported by independent academic work. The paper uses the term because it has become the most widely adopted vocabulary for the agent-specific case; the underlying analytical methodology is older.

The distinction between toxic flows and the lethal trifecta is operationally important. The trifecta names a configuration: an agent that has all three capabilities (sensitive data access, untrusted input exposure, egress channel) is exposed to a class of attack. Toxic flows names an *analysis*: the systematic examination of an agent's authorised tool inventory to identify specific paths from untrusted input through privileged data access to egress. A trifecta-configuration agent has many possible toxic flows; not all of them are equally exploitable; and the analysis identifies which specific paths warrant remediation versus which are theoretically present but practically blocked by other controls.

The technical method underlying toxic-flow analysis is graph-based. The agent's authorised tools are nodes; the relationships among tools — which tool's outputs can flow into which other tool's inputs, given the agent's reasoning patterns — are edges. A flow from a node receiving untrusted input to a node performing egress, traversing nodes that access sensitive data, is a toxic flow. The analysis is static in that it operates on the declared tool inventory rather than on observed runtime behaviour, which means it can identify potential flows before they have been demonstrated; it is also incomplete in that it cannot reason perfectly about whether the agent's reasoning patterns will actually traverse a given path. Static toxic-flow analysis identifies the surface area of compositional risk; runtime monitoring is required to confirm that the surface is being exploited or to confirm that other controls (e.g., human review at specific steps) prevent traversal in practice.

The evidentiary basis for treating toxic flows as both a real threat and an addressable one rests on three strands. First, the empirical reality that compositional attacks are exploitable across MCP-integrated agent configurations is supported by MCPTox's findings on tool-poisoning susceptibility (cited in §3.2.1), since metadata-layer attacks are themselves one input to the broader compositional surface. Second, static compositional analysis applied to agent tool chains is supported by Snyk Agent Scan's productisation following the Invariant Labs acquisition: the TF001 issue class and the `--full-toxic-flows` analysis option are the most operationally specified instances of the methodology in commercial tooling as of early 2026. Third, the paper's own contribution is the integration of compositional analysis into regulator-legible assurance — the synthesis framework treats static toxic-flow analysis as a required component, with outputs structured for supervisory presentation, rather than as an optional advanced capability or a developer-tooling concern.

The pattern's implications for the existing disciplines parallel those of the lethal trifecta. Cybersecurity's per-action evaluation cannot detect compositional risk. AI governance specifies adversarial testing without specifying compositional testing as a distinct requirement. Model risk management has no concept of inter-system composition. What is novel about toxic-flow analysis is not the underlying technique — taint analysis is decades old — but its specific application to agent tool chains, which existing static-analysis tooling does not natively perform.

### 3.2.5 Zero-click exfiltration

*Zero-click exfiltration* describes attacks in which the agent processes content in the background, without any human interaction at the moment of execution, and that content triggers exfiltration of sensitive data. The pattern compounds ASI01 (Agent Goal Hijack) and ASI09 (Human-Agent Trust Exploitation), and exploits the architectural property *semantic attack susceptibility* in combination with deployment configurations that authorise the agent to process content asynchronously.

The architectural fact the pattern exploits is that agent deployments commonly authorise the agent to process content in background contexts: monitored inboxes, indexed document stores, scheduled jobs, calendar entries, or task-tracking systems. The legitimate use case is automation: the agent processes content as part of routine work without requiring user interaction for each step. The attack co-opts the same infrastructure: an attacker places malicious content into the monitored corpus, and the agent processes it on its normal schedule, executing the embedded instructions without any human present to intervene.

The pattern breaks an assumption embedded in many agent security models: that the user is in the loop at the moment of action and can intervene. The assumption is false for zero-click attacks by definition. The user authorised the agent to monitor the inbox; the user did not authorise each specific email to be processed in any particular way; the attack arrives as part of the authorised monitoring stream and executes before any human review.

EchoLeak (June 2025) is the canonical zero-click case in production agent systems. AgentFlayer (August 2025) extended the pattern across multiple platforms. Operant AI's *Shadow Escape* disclosure of 22 October 2025 (*Grade C*: vendor disclosure without independent technical corroboration at the time of writing)[^operant-source] demonstrated zero-click exfiltration specifically through MCP, framed by the disclosing firm as "the first zero-click agentic attack via MCP," cross-platform across ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini, with sensitive personal data (Social Security numbers, medical record numbers, financial data) as the exfiltration target. Johann Rehberger's *Month of AI Bugs* (August 2025) (*Grade B*: independent researcher disclosure with vendor acknowledgements) published over twenty zero-click and prompt-injection disclosures across major agentic AI coding assistants — including ChatGPT, Cursor, Amp, Devin, OpenHands, Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, and Google Jules — establishing the pattern by mid-2025 as a recurrent disclosed pattern rather than an exceptional finding. The evidence base remains disclosure-heavy; the rate of disclosure across diverse platforms supports the claim that the pattern is general rather than platform-specific.

[^operant-source]: The Shadow Escape disclosure is authored by the disclosing security firm and uses the firm's framing of priority claims. Earlier disclosures — EchoLeak in June, AgentFlayer in August, Rehberger's earlier work — establish that zero-click attacks predate Shadow Escape; the disclosure's priority claim should be read as referring specifically to MCP-mediated zero-click attacks rather than to zero-click attacks in general.

The defence of zero-click attacks is architecturally constrained. Human-in-the-loop controls — requiring user approval before specific actions — break the legitimate use case of background processing if applied broadly, and provide no defence if applied narrowly. Runtime policy gates that examine outbound actions for risk indicators offer partial defence but cannot perfectly distinguish legitimate automation from malicious composition. Content filtering on the input side — examining inbox content for instruction-like patterns — is the best defence currently available for most deployments, but is incomplete: an attacker can construct content that does not pattern-match as instructions while still functioning as instructions to the model. The trade-off between automation utility and zero-click risk is architectural, and the synthesis framework treats it as a deployment-level decision that requires explicit risk acceptance rather than as a problem soluble through any single control.

The pattern's relationship to the existing disciplines: cybersecurity addresses inbound threats through perimeter controls and outbound through DLP, but neither addresses the case where the threat enters through legitimate channels and operates on the agent's reasoning. AI governance specifies adversarial-input testing without specifying the asynchronous-processing case. Model risk management does not engage the threat model. The gap is again where architectural properties of agents — semantic attack susceptibility combined with background-processing configurations — produce threats the disciplines were not constructed to address.

## 3.3 Financial-services amplifications

The patterns examined in §3.2 are not specific to financial services; they affect any agent deployment with the relevant architectural properties and configured authorities. The argument of this sub-section is that financial services operates under conditions that amplify each pattern's consequences and concentrate the assurance challenge in ways that justify treating agent assurance in financial services as a distinct discipline rather than as an application of generic agent security.

Five amplifications recur across the regulated financial-services context.

**Delegated transactional authority.** Financial-services agents deployed in production routinely operate in workflows with payment, underwriting, claims, trading, or advisory authority. The consequence surface of a successful attack is qualitatively different from the consequence surface in general enterprise deployments. An advisory agent compromise produces bad recommendations; a transactional agent compromise produces moved capital. The transactional case is also frequently irreversible within operational time frames: payments cleared, trades executed, and underwriting decisions communicated cannot be reversed by detection of the compromise alone, and the institution's recovery posture must accommodate that irreversibility.

**Concurrent data sensitivity regimes.** A single incident in a regulated financial-services agent typically triggers obligations under multiple disclosure regimes simultaneously: the General Data Protection Regulation's Articles 33 (notification of personal data breach to the supervisory authority within 72 hours) and 34 (communication of a personal data breach to the data subject) for personal-data breaches, national banking secrecy regimes (the German *Bankgeheimnis*, the Swiss *Bankkundengeheimnis*, equivalent regimes in other jurisdictions), Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard requirements where payment data is involved, MiFID II recordkeeping obligations, e-money and payment-services obligations, and in bancassurance configurations, healthcare-privacy regimes for life and health insurance data. The remediation cost compounds across regimes; each regime has its own timeline, its own disclosure expectations, and its own supervisory consequences. An incident that would be a single notification event for a general enterprise becomes a multi-regime disclosure exercise for a regulated institution.

**Supervisory scrutiny convergence.** The AI Act's Annex III classifies creditworthiness assessment and credit scoring (Section 5(b)) and life-and-health insurance pricing and risk assessment (Section 5(c)) as high-risk applications, bringing them within the Act's high-risk obligation regime. DORA has applied to in-scope financial entities since January 2025 and brings AI deployments within its ICT-risk and operational-resilience scope. National supervisors — BaFin (Germany), ACPR (France), DNB (Netherlands), CSSF (Luxembourg), and others — have published increasingly specific AI-related guidance through 2024 and 2025. EIOPA (through Opinion BoS-25-360) and the European Central Bank's Single Supervisory Mechanism (through *Supervisory Priorities 2026–2028*, Priority 2) have established institutional engagement signalling sustained supervisory attention. A regulated financial-services agent deployment faces examination risk under multiple concurrent regimes, and the regimes are not independent: a finding under one frequently triggers inquiry under others.

**Correlated-behaviour macroprudential risk.** Where multiple financial-services institutions deploy agents using similar foundation models and similar tool configurations, the institutions may exhibit correlated responses to the same adversarial inputs. The Financial Stability Board's *The Financial Stability Implications of Artificial Intelligence* (14 November 2024), the Bank of England Financial Policy Committee's published statements on AI in the financial system through 2025, the European Central Bank's *Financial Stability Review* coverage of AI-related vulnerabilities, the International Monetary Fund's *Global Financial Stability Report* (October 2024), and the Bank for International Settlements' working paper series on AI supply-chain concentration, among others, have flagged concentration and correlation as macroprudential concerns for AI-reliant financial systems. Simulation-based research on AI-driven correlated trading behaviour supports the analytical concern, though the empirical evidence remains limited. The implication for agent assurance is that the consequence surface of an incident extends beyond the affected institution: correlated failure across multiple institutions is a class of risk individual-firm controls do not address, and the macroprudential dimension is part of why supervisors are paying attention.

**Reputational asymmetry.** A general-enterprise chatbot incident is, in most cases, a footnote: technical-press coverage, a vendor patch, a corrective action. A regulated-financial-services agent incident is, by contrast, frequently a stock-impacting event for the affected institution: the institution's name appears in mainstream financial press, supervisory inquiries follow, and customer-trust impacts are slow to recover. The asymmetry is compounded by the political salience of AI in finance, by media attention to AI-related failures, and by supervisors' tendency to broaden inquiries in response to public incidents. The reputational consequence is not proportional to the technical severity of the incident, and that asymmetry is part of why supervisory and second-line attention to agent deployments is justified.

These amplifications are not merely matters of degree. Taken together, they constitute the case for treating agent assurance in financial services as a distinct discipline. The technical patterns are general; the consequence surface, the regulatory regime, the macroprudential dimension, and the reputational impact are specific to financial services and require methodology that addresses them on their own terms.

## 3.4 Bridge to Section 4

The threat taxonomy this section developed serves as the empirical referent against which the synthesis framework Section 4 articulates its design principles. The principles — evidence-first, framework-anchored, regulator-legible, composable and continuous, and principal-bound — are not abstract preferences; each responds to a specific class of threat above. *Evidence-first* responds to the runtime-constructed reasoning context that makes attacks like tool poisoning detectable only with deliberate context capture. *Framework-anchored* responds to the regulatory-legibility requirement the financial-services amplifications make non-negotiable. *Regulator-legible* responds to the supervisory-scrutiny dimension materialised through 2024–2025. *Composable and continuous* responds to the toxic-flow and lethal-trifecta patterns whose compositional character defeats per-action controls. *Principal-bound* responds to the identity-and-privilege-abuse pattern whose foundation is the gap between agent and requesting-principal authority. Section 4 develops each principle in turn, with the threat patterns above as concrete referents.
# Section 4: A Synthesis Framework

The synthesis framework this section develops is a methodology for the assurance of autonomous AI agents in regulated financial services. It is not a replacement for the disciplines examined in Section 2; it is a structured composition of their contributions, augmented at the points the threat taxonomy in Section 3 has shown those disciplines individually cannot reach. The framework's claim is not novelty in any of its constituent components — every element has antecedents in established practice — but in the specific composition: five design principles, jointly necessary, applied to the architectural properties of agents identified in Section 1, anchored in the regulatory and standards stack the institutions deploying them already operate under.

The framework is preliminary in an important sense. It has been developed against the public record of agent deployments, supervisory communications, and security disclosures, and applied to public reference deployments. It has not yet been validated through application to live regulated production environments at scale. The empirical evidence base for several of its claims will accumulate over the engagement period the broader project envisages. The section says so where it matters, identifies the specific places extension or empirical validation is required, and frames its claims to what the work has actually established.

The section is structured in four parts. §4.1 articulates the five design principles. §4.2 specifies the control matrix that operationalises the principles. §4.3 walks through illustrative controls — one per threat pattern from §3.2 — to demonstrate the matrix in use. §4.4 closes with an honest accounting of what the framework requires that it does not yet deliver.

## 4.1 Five design principles

The framework rests on five design principles. Each is the response to a specific architectural property identified in §1.4 and a specific threat pattern documented in §3.2; each is the answer to a question the existing disciplines examined in Section 2 do not natively address. The principles are not preferences; they are the constraints any methodology that addresses agent assurance in regulated financial services must satisfy. A methodology that satisfies fewer than all five is, in the framework's view, insufficient for the task.

### Evidence-first

The first principle requires that every control in the framework produce a verifiable artifact captured at runtime. The motivation is the architectural property of *runtime-constructed reasoning context*: an agent's behaviour is determined by reasoning over inputs assembled during execution and ephemeral by default; what is not captured at runtime cannot be reconstructed at audit time. The threat patterns in §3.2 — particularly tool poisoning, the lethal trifecta, and zero-click exfiltration — operate by manipulating runtime context, and detection of such manipulation requires visibility into what the model actually saw. Procedural attestation that the institution intends to capture context is not equivalent to evidence that the context was captured.

The principle has two operational implications. First, the framework rejects controls that cannot be paired with a specific evidence artifact: if a proposed control cannot answer the question "what does the institution produce to demonstrate the control is operating?" the control is incomplete and is not adopted into the matrix. Second, the framework treats evidence capture as a design property of the agent rather than as an operational overlay added after deployment; logging architecture is required to be addressed at architecture time, with retention and integrity controls specified per the type of evidence and the regulatory regime under which it is captured.

A consequential caveat. Runtime context captured for after-the-fact reconstruction may contain personal data, customer secrets, confidential business information, legally privileged communications, or content otherwise subject to data-protection or sectoral confidentiality regimes. The evidence-first principle therefore requires, as a non-negotiable corollary, that evidence capture be (i) data-minimised — only what is necessary to support the assurance objective, and structured to permit the most sensitive content to be redacted or excluded; (ii) access-controlled — evidence repositories protected with the same rigour as the underlying systems; (iii) encrypted in transit and at rest; (iv) retention-governed — retained for the period the assurance and regulatory regime require and no longer; and (v) privacy-reviewed — subject to data-protection-by-design review under GDPR Article 25 and any relevant sectoral confidentiality obligations. Evidence-first does not authorise comprehensive runtime logging by default; it requires a designed evidence-capture architecture whose privacy and security properties are themselves documented and assured.

The principle's lineage is in the international assurance standards — ISAE 3000 (Revised) issued by the IAASB, and AT-C 205 issued by the AICPA — which structure assurance engagements around evidence-based reasonable or limited assurance. The framework adopts their disciplinary structure without claiming to deliver formal attestation, which requires a qualified signatory the framework does not constitute. The framework's outputs are *assurance-ready evidence*: artifacts structured so that, where a qualified practitioner relationship exists, they can support formal attestation, and where one does not, they can support the institution's own assurance posture and supervisory engagement.

### Framework-anchored

The second principle requires that every control map to named regulatory and standards anchors — specific articles of the AI Act, specific articles of DORA, specific clauses of ISO 42001's Annex A, specific functions of the NIST AI RMF, specific OWASP ASI categories. The motivation is twofold. First, deployers in regulated financial services are subject to the regulatory regime regardless of the framework's existence; controls that satisfy the framework's requirements without also satisfying regulatory obligations create work that does not retire risk. Controls anchored to specific regulatory articles do both at once. Second, supervisors and second-line risk leaders examine controls against the regimes they themselves operate under. A control justified by reference to "best practice" or to a vendor-branded methodology is harder to defend, in supervisory examination, than a control justified by reference to AI Act Article 15 and DORA Article 28. Framework anchoring is what makes the methodology supervisor-legible at the level of individual controls rather than only at the level of overall posture.

The principle's lineage is in the cross-walking practice that has been established for cybersecurity controls: the Cloud Security Alliance Cloud Controls Matrix (CSA CCM), NIST SP 800-53's published mapping to ISO/IEC 27001, and the various CIS Controls cross-walks to NIST CSF and to ISO 27001. The CCM did not invent cloud security; it organised existing standards into a matrix that buyers could map to their own compliance obligations. The framework's control matrix inherits this structure deliberately. The matrix's columns include AI Act articles, DORA articles, ISO 42001 clauses, NIST AI RMF functions, OWASP ASI categories, and the test procedures and evidence artifacts that operationalise each control.

### Regulator-legible

The third principle requires that every output of the framework be readable by supervisory staff without translation. The motivation is the supervisory-scrutiny dimension documented in §3.3: a regulated financial-services agent deployment faces examination risk under multiple concurrent regimes, and the institution's ability to demonstrate compliance depends in significant part on whether the documentation it produces can be engaged with by supervisors using the vocabulary they themselves operate under. Outputs structured around proprietary control IDs, vendor-specific methodology language, or assessor-internal categorisations require translation work that adds friction to supervisory engagement and reduces the institution's effectiveness in examinations.

The principle has three operational implications. Findings are framed using regulatory vocabulary (AI Act articles, DORA articles, OWASP ASI categories) rather than internal taxonomies. Evidence is organised to map directly to regulatory citation patterns, so a supervisor examining the institution's documentation can locate evidence against any specific obligation without re-categorisation. Reports are structured to be forwardable to a supervisor without redaction or reframing. The principle does not require that all outputs be designed for supervisors specifically; reports for second-line consumption are different from reports for board consumption, and both are different from material the supervisor would actually request. The principle requires that the assurance-ready evidence underlying these documents is structured for supervisory legibility from the outset, so that any of the documents can be produced from it without translation.

The principle's lineage is in the threat-led penetration testing frameworks: CBEST (Bank of England, 2014, with the PRA, FCA, and FMID supervisory community) and TIBER-EU (ECB, May 2018), which were designed from the outset to produce supervisor-consumable outputs. DORA's Articles 24–27 — covering the digital operational resilience testing programme generally (Art. 24), basic testing requirements (Art. 25), and advanced testing through threat-led penetration testing (Arts. 26 and 27) — extend the pattern to the European level. The framework's outputs are designed to the same disposition: regulator-legibility is a design property, not a translation step.

### Composable and continuous

The fourth principle requires that the framework adapt across agent architectures (MCP-based, non-MCP, hybrid) and that assurance be ongoing rather than point-in-time. The motivation is in two architectural properties — *composite tool chains* and the dynamic character of agent deployments — and in two threat patterns — the lethal trifecta and toxic flows — that operate through compositions of capabilities rather than through individual components.

The composability requirement reflects that agent architectures are not standardised. MCP is the most widely-adopted agent-tooling standard as of the paper's writing, but it is one convention among several; firms also deploy agents using LangGraph configurations, custom architectures, hybrid approaches, and proprietary platforms. A framework addressing only MCP deployments does not assure an institution deploying agents in any other configuration. The framework's controls are therefore expressed at an architectural abstraction layer: "tool metadata integrity" rather than "MCP server metadata signature verification." Implementation-specific guidance lives below the abstraction layer; the framework can be applied to non-MCP deployments by adapting the implementation guidance while preserving the control structure.

The continuity requirement reflects that agent deployments change over time. A configuration assured in March 2026 is not necessarily the same configuration in April 2026: tool inventories evolve, foundation-model versions update, deployment policies are revised. Point-in-time assessments — analogous to a SOC 2 Type I audit — certify a snapshot. The framework requires that assurance track configuration changes, with explicit specification of the frequency at which each control's evidence must be regenerated. Some controls (architectural decisions, governance documentation) require annual review; some (tool inventory, control mapping) require per-deployment refresh; some (runtime monitoring, anomaly detection) require continuous operation. The framework's matrix specifies frequency per control, and the engagement methodology accommodates the cadence each control requires.

The principle's lineage is in management-system thinking — ISO 42001's PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) cycles, NIST AI RMF's lifecycle approach, the ECB Guide to Internal Models' continuous-validation requirement. The framework inherits the disposition rather than inventing it.

### Principal-bound

The fifth principle requires that identity and authority be enforced at the interaction level — per-request, per-principal — rather than at the service level. The motivation is the *capacity for delegated authority* property and the *identity and privilege abuse* threat pattern. An agent that holds pooled service credentials with broad authority, exercising those credentials on behalf of many users without principal propagation through the tool chain, is exposed to a class of attack the existing cybersecurity disciplines do not natively address; the framework's position is that this architecture is not acceptable for regulated financial-services deployments without compensating controls that re-establish principal binding at the interaction layer.

The principle has three operational implications. First, for remote and protected MCP server deployments in regulated financial services, the framework requires conformance with the MCP authorization profile articulated in the protocol's June 2025 revision: MCP servers acting as OAuth 2.1 Resource Servers, audience validation under RFC 9068, resource indicators under RFC 8707, and prohibition of token pass-through to upstream APIs. The framework requires this conformance for regulated deployments even though the protocol itself describes authorization as optional, and recommends that stdio and other local-transport configurations specify equivalent controls separately. Second, where principal propagation cannot be implemented natively (because the upstream system does not accept propagated identity), the deployment must implement scope-narrowing controls that limit the agent's effective authority on a per-request basis to what the requesting principal could have asked for directly. Third, just-in-time credential issuance — credentials minted for the specific request and scoped to its specific authority — is preferred over standing service credentials wherever the architecture supports it.

The principle's lineage is in zero-trust security architectures — NIST SP 800-207 (Zero Trust Architecture, August 2020) is the canonical reference — which established the disposition of enforcing identity and authority at the interaction layer rather than at the network or service perimeter. The framework extends the disposition into the agent tool-invocation layer, where current MCP deployments commonly treat the agent as a service account rather than as a delegated actor. The extension is not novel in concept; it is the application of zero-trust thinking to a layer where its application has been incomplete.

### How the principles interlock

The five principles are not independent constraints; they presuppose and reinforce one another. *Evidence-first* without *regulator-legibility* produces evidence the institution cannot use in supervisory engagement. *Framework-anchored* without *evidence-first* produces controls justified by reference to regulation but not demonstrably operating. *Composable and continuous* without *principal-bound* produces a methodology that adapts across architectures but fails to address the core architectural concern of agent deployments. *Principal-bound* without *framework-anchored* produces controls justified by zero-trust thinking but not legible to supervisors operating under DORA. Each principle is necessary, none is sufficient, and the methodology requires all five.

This interlock is the framework's structural argument. Existing disciplines, examined individually, contribute one or two principles in their fullest forms — model risk management contributes evidence discipline at the level of model validation, AI governance contributes framework-anchoring at the organisational level, cybersecurity contributes principal-binding at the network and identity layers. None of the disciplines, individually or in straightforward composition, contributes all five at the level of agent deployments. The synthesis is the contribution.

## 4.2 The control matrix

The control matrix operationalises the principles. Each control in the matrix is a row; each column captures a specific dimension of how the control is justified, implemented, tested, and evidenced.

The matrix's column structure is as follows:

- **Control ID.** A stable identifier for the control, structured to indicate the threat class the control addresses (e.g., TP-01 for the first tool-poisoning control).
- **Control Statement.** A precise declarative statement of what the control requires, framed at the architectural abstraction layer rather than the implementation layer.
- **OWASP ASI Reference.** The OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications categories the control addresses, including compositional references where the control addresses cross-cutting patterns.
- **AI Act Article.** The specific AI Act article(s) the control is anchored to.
- **DORA Article.** The specific DORA article(s) the control is anchored to.
- **ISO 42001 Reference.** The ISO 42001 Annex A clauses the control implements or aligns with.
- **NIST AI RMF Function.** The NIST AI RMF function (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) the control supports.
- **Test Procedure.** The specific testing methodology used to verify the control is operating, expressed concretely enough to be reproducible.
- **Evidence Artifact.** The specific artifact the control produces as evidence of its operation, structured for assurance use.
- **Frequency.** The cadence at which the control's evidence must be regenerated — annual, per-deployment, per-session, continuous.
- **Ownership.** The institutional function accountable for the control's operation and for the production and integrity of its evidence artifact. Typical assignments span first-line operations, second-line risk and compliance, third-line internal audit, the CISO function, the model-risk function, legal and privacy functions, and vendor-management functions, with most controls jointly owned across functions and a single function designated as accountable.

A subtlety in the regulatory and standards anchoring deserves explicit treatment. Different controls relate to their cited regulatory anchors with different strengths, and the framework distinguishes four such strengths so the anchoring is not made to bear more weight than the underlying article actually carries:

- **Direct.** The cited article explicitly addresses the control domain, and the control implements the article's specific requirement.
- **Indirect.** The article supports the control through a broader obligation, of which the control's domain is one part among others.
- **Analogical.** The article is a defensible anchor — the control is consistent with the article's logic and would plausibly be examined under the article — but the article does not itself specify the control's mechanism.
- **Gap.** The control fills a gap not addressed by any of the cited anchors; the citation is to articles in the same general domain rather than to articles that specifically authorise or require the control.

Controls in the matrix carry a strength label for each cited anchor; the illustrative controls in §4.3 carry the labels in their tabular presentation. This discipline allows the framework's regulatory legibility to be evaluated by a reader who would otherwise take undifferentiated citations as overclaim, and forces the framework's authors to be explicit about where the work of each anchor actually ends.

The matrix's full population — covering the synthesis framework's complete control set — appears in Appendix B. The body of the paper presents five illustrative controls in §4.3, one per threat pattern from §3.2, sufficient to demonstrate the matrix in use.

The matrix is not exhaustive, and is not intended to be. The synthesis framework's contribution is the structure: the column architecture, the control derivation logic, the principle-to-control traceability, and the evidence-and-test specification. Specific controls will be revised as the methodology is applied to live deployments and as the regulatory landscape evolves; the matrix is a living artifact. The version published with the paper is v1.0 of an ongoing artifact, with revision protocols specified in the appendices.

## 4.3 Illustrative controls

The five controls below — one per threat pattern examined in §3.2 — demonstrate the matrix in use. Each control's full row in the matrix is presented; the prose around each control explains the control's derivation and its connection to the design principles.

### TP-01: Tool metadata integrity verification

**Statement.** Tool metadata (descriptions, schemas, parameter definitions, output formats) consumed by the agent must be cryptographically pinned to a specific version and verified against an internal registry of approved tool definitions at session initialisation; modifications to tool metadata between session initialisations require re-approval before the modified metadata is consumed.

**OWASP ASI references.** ASI04 (Agentic Supply Chain Vulnerabilities); ASI02 (Tool Misuse).

**Regulatory anchors.** AI Act Article 15 (accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity) — *Indirect* (the article requires resilience to errors, faults, and inconsistencies and protection against attempts by unauthorised third parties to alter use or performance, which encompasses tool-metadata integrity but does not address it specifically); DORA Article 28 (general principles for ICT third-party risk management) — *Analogical* (third-party MCP server providers fall within the definition of ICT third-party service providers, but the metadata-poisoning vector is not specifically addressed in the Article).

**Standards references.** ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Annex A.6.2 (third-party relationships) — *Indirect* (covers supplier relationships in the AI context, applicable to MCP-server suppliers but without metadata-specific guidance); NIST AI RMF Manage 2 (managing risks from third-party AI systems and resources) — *Indirect* (manages risks from third-party AI components including supply-chain considerations but without metadata-poisoning specificity).

**Test procedure.** At each session initialisation, the agent's MCP client computes a cryptographic hash of each consumed tool's metadata and verifies the hash against the internal registry. Hashes that do not match registered values cause session initialisation to fail with a documented exception. Periodic adversarial testing introduces metadata variants to verify the verification step operates.

**Evidence artifact.** Per-session log entry recording the hashes of all consumed tool metadata, with timestamps, registry versions, and verification outcomes. Retained per the institution's logging-retention policy.

**Frequency.** Per-session.

**Ownership.** First-line: agent operations team accountable for MCP integration. Second-line: ICT risk function for the third-party-supply-chain dimension; vendor-management function for vendor-MCP-server change-control. Joint with CISO for cryptographic-pinning infrastructure. Designated accountable: ICT risk.

The control derives from the *evidence-first* and *framework-anchored* principles. The evidence-first principle requires that the verification step be demonstrable in runtime evidence, not merely policy-attested; the per-session log entry is the artifact. The framework-anchored principle requires the regulatory and standards anchoring; AI Act Article 15 and DORA Article 28 supply it. The control's design responds to the metadata-layer tool-poisoning pattern specifically — pinning and verifying the metadata the agent reads at session initialisation, before the model's reasoning treats that metadata as instructions. Tool-output content attacks, in which a tool returns runtime content that manipulates the agent's reasoning, are not addressed by TP-01; those are addressed by the lethal-trifecta and zero-click controls (LT-01 and ZC-01) below, where compositional review and runtime gating respond to the threat at the layers where it operates.

### IA-01: Principal propagation through tool chains

**Statement.** Where the agent invokes tools that operate on principal-specific resources, the requesting principal's identity must be propagated through the tool chain such that downstream resource access is authorised against the requesting principal's authority rather than against the agent's authority. Where principal propagation is not architecturally possible, the agent must implement scope-narrowing controls that limit per-request authority to what the requesting principal could have requested directly.

**OWASP ASI references.** ASI03 (Identity & Privilege Abuse).

**Regulatory anchors.** AI Act Article 14 (human oversight) — *Analogical*; AI Act Article 15 (cybersecurity) — *Indirect*; DORA Article 9 (protection and prevention) — *Indirect*.

**Standards references.** ISO 42001 Annex A.5.5 (information security) — *Indirect*; NIST AI RMF Manage (MP-5) — *Indirect*; NIST SP 800-207 (Zero Trust Architecture) — *Direct*.

**Test procedure.** Adversarial testing constructs requests in which the requesting principal lacks authority for the action the agent would otherwise be authorised to perform. Tests verify that the agent's response either propagates the requesting principal's identity to the downstream system (which then denies the request on its own controls) or applies scope-narrowing logic that prevents the request from reaching the downstream system. Tests are repeated across the deployment's full inventory of tool chains.

**Evidence artifact.** Per-tool-chain documentation of principal propagation mechanism (or scope-narrowing alternative), supplemented by adversarial test logs demonstrating the mechanism's operation.

**Frequency.** Per-deployment for documentation; quarterly for adversarial testing.

**Ownership.** First-line: agent architecture and identity-management teams jointly. Second-line: ICT risk and operational risk for principal-model design; legal/privacy for personal-data implications of scope-narrowing logic. Designated accountable: CISO function (for identity architecture).

The control derives from the *principal-bound* and *evidence-first* principles. The principal-bound principle requires that authority flow with the principal rather than aggregating in the agent; the control's design implements the principle at the tool-chain layer. The evidence-first principle requires runtime verification through adversarial testing.

### LT-01: Lethal trifecta architectural review

**Statement.** Agent configurations in which the agent simultaneously has access to sensitive data, exposure to untrusted input, and use of an egress channel must undergo architectural review before deployment, with explicit acceptance of the residual risk by an accountable executive. Where compensating controls (human review at specific steps, content filtering, runtime gating) are intended to mitigate the configuration risk, the controls' effectiveness must be tested against documented threat scenarios prior to acceptance.

**OWASP ASI references.** ASI01 (Agent Goal Hijack) + ASI02 (Tool Misuse) + ASI03 (Identity & Privilege Abuse), in compositional reference.

**Regulatory anchors.** AI Act Article 15 (cybersecurity, robustness) — *Indirect*; AI Act Article 9 (risk management) — *Indirect*; DORA Article 5 (ICT risk management framework) — *Indirect*.

**Standards references.** Cross-cuts ISO 42001 (no single clause) — *Gap*; NIST AI RMF Map (MP-1) and Manage (MG-3, MG-4) — *Indirect*.

**Test procedure.** Pre-deployment threat-modelling exercise enumerates the agent's data-access, input-exposure, and egress-channel inventory, identifies trifecta configurations, and tests proposed compensating controls against documented threat scenarios derived from public disclosures (EchoLeak, AgentFlayer, Shadow Escape, others as relevant).

**Evidence artifact.** Architectural review document signed by accountable executive, including the trifecta inventory, compensating-control design, test results, and explicit residual-risk acceptance.

**Frequency.** Per-deployment, with re-review on material configuration change.

**Ownership.** First-line: deployment owner and architecture team. Second-line: enterprise risk function for accountability artifact, with explicit CRO sign-off on residual-risk acceptance. Joint with CISO and model-risk function for technical review. Designated accountable: CRO.

The control derives from the *composable and continuous* and *evidence-first* principles. The compositional analysis is the response to the lethal trifecta's compositional character; the architectural review document is the evidence the institution can present to demonstrate the compositional risk has been examined and accepted. The principle of accountability at the executive level is intentional: trifecta configurations are sometimes operationally necessary, and the framework's position is that they require explicit, named acceptance rather than implicit acceptance through deployment.

### TF-01: Toxic-flow analysis of authorised tool inventory

**Statement.** The agent's authorised tool inventory must undergo static toxic-flow analysis prior to production deployment, identifying paths from untrusted-input nodes through privileged-data-access nodes to egress nodes; identified flows must be reviewed against deployment configurations and either remediated, gated by compensating controls, or accepted as residual risk by an accountable executive.

**OWASP ASI references.** ASI02 (Tool Misuse) + ASI04 (Agentic Supply Chain Vulnerabilities), in compositional reference.

**Regulatory anchors.** AI Act Article 15 (cybersecurity) — *Indirect*; AI Act Article 11 (technical documentation) — *Analogical*; DORA Article 25 (digital operational resilience testing) — *Indirect*; DORA Article 5 (ICT risk) — *Indirect*.

**Standards references.** ISO 42001 Annex A.8.1 (operational security) — *Indirect*; A.7 (third party) — *Indirect*; NIST AI RMF Measure (MS-1) — *Indirect*; Manage (MG-3) — *Indirect*.

**Test procedure.** Static analysis of the deployment's tool inventory using compositional-analysis tooling (Snyk Agent Scan TF001 or equivalent functionality, where the institution's procurement and vendor-relationship policies permit; otherwise equivalent in-house implementation). Analysis output is reviewed jointly by the deployment's technical and risk leadership.

**Evidence artifact.** Toxic-flow analysis report with identified flows, severity classifications, remediation or compensating-control decisions, and residual-risk acceptance documentation.

**Frequency.** Per-deployment; re-analysis on material change to tool inventory or configuration.

**Ownership.** First-line: deployment technical leadership for the tool-inventory specification. Second-line: ICT risk function for the analytical review and residual-risk acceptance; vendor-management function for tools sourced from third parties. Designated accountable: ICT risk function.

The control derives from the *composable and continuous* principle directly: compositional analysis is the response to compositional threats. The framework-anchored principle is also engaged because the regulatory anchoring (AI Act Article 15, DORA Article 25) makes the analysis a regulatory-compliance artifact in addition to a security artifact.

### ZC-01: Asynchronous-processing risk gating

**Statement.** Agents authorised to process content in background or asynchronous contexts (monitored inboxes, indexed document stores, scheduled jobs) must implement runtime policy gates on outbound tool invocations triggered by such content, with policies derived from documented threat scenarios for zero-click exfiltration.

**OWASP ASI references.** ASI01 (Agent Goal Hijack) + ASI09 (Human-Agent Trust Exploitation), in compositional reference.

**Regulatory anchors.** AI Act Article 14 (human oversight) — *Indirect* (requires human oversight measures appropriate to the system's risks but does not specify zero-click attack patterns); AI Act Article 15 (accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity) — *Indirect* (encompasses cybersecurity protection but not zero-click composition specifically); DORA Article 9 (protection and prevention) — *Indirect* (requires ICT security measures protecting against unauthorised access, but written for code-execution rather than agent-mediated exfiltration); DORA Articles 17–18 (ICT-related incident management process and incident classification) — *Analogical* (the management process applies once an incident is detected, but the zero-click pattern's distinguishing feature is detection failure, which Articles 17–18 do not specifically address).

**Standards references.** ISO 42001 Annex A.8.3 (human oversight) — *Indirect*; A.5.5 (information security) — *Indirect*; NIST AI RMF Govern (GV-3) — *Indirect*; Manage (MG-4) — *Indirect*.

**Test procedure.** Adversarial testing introduces content with embedded instructions into the agent's monitored corpus and verifies that runtime policy gates either prevent the agent from acting on the embedded instructions or escalate the proposed action for human review. Tests cover a range of content types and instruction patterns derived from public disclosures.

**Evidence artifact.** Runtime gate configuration documentation; per-incident logs of gate activations; periodic testing reports demonstrating gate effectiveness.

**Frequency.** Per-deployment for configuration; continuous for runtime logs; quarterly for adversarial testing.

**Ownership.** First-line: agent operations team and security operations centre jointly. Second-line: ICT risk function for runtime-policy specification and review; legal/privacy for evidence-capture content review. Designated accountable: CISO function.

The control derives from the *evidence-first* and *principal-bound* principles in combination. Evidence-first requires runtime demonstration that the gate operates; principal-bound is engaged because the gate's purpose is to preserve the requesting principal's authority over the agent's actions even in asynchronous contexts where no principal is present at the moment of action.

## 4.4 What the framework requires that it does not yet deliver

The framework's contribution is the structure: the principles, the control matrix architecture, the derivation logic from threats and properties to controls, and the cross-walking that makes the controls regulator-legible. The framework's contribution is not an exhaustive control set, an empirically validated effectiveness assessment, or a turnkey methodology that requires no adaptation. Three honest acknowledgements close the section.

**Empirical validation is incomplete.** The framework has been developed against the public record and applied to public reference deployments. The control set has not been validated through application to live regulated production environments at scale. The patterns established in §3.2 are well-evidenced; the controls in §4.3 are derived logically from those patterns, but their effectiveness in live deployments — false-positive rates, false-negative rates, operational overhead, integration complexity — is empirically under-characterised. The engagement period the broader project's plan envisages is precisely the period during which this empirical evidence is generated; the framework will be revised on the basis of what is learned.

**The full control matrix is forthcoming.** This section presented five illustrative controls. The full matrix, covering the synthesis framework's complete control set, appears in Appendix B and is itself v1.0 of an ongoing artifact. Subsequent revisions will incorporate additional controls as the methodology is applied to deployments with configurations not represented in the v1.0 set, particularly non-MCP architectures and hybrid deployments. The matrix's revision protocol — versioning, change documentation, public commitment to revision frequency — is specified in the appendix.

**Attestation requires partnership.** The framework produces *assurance-ready evidence*: artifacts structured to support assurance engagements where qualified practitioners are involved. The framework does not itself constitute attestation, certification, or rating, and does not represent the firm as authorised to deliver such functions. Where institutions deploying the framework's methodology require formal attestation, the engagement requires partnership with a qualified ISAE 3000 or AT-C 205 practitioner. The framework's design supports such partnerships by structuring outputs to be useable as input to formal assurance engagements.

The framework's strongest claim is therefore narrow and defensible. It claims to be a coherent, operationally specified, regulator-legible methodology for the assurance of AI agents in regulated financial services, derived from the empirical record and the regulatory architecture in force. It claims to address the architectural properties of agents and the threat patterns documented in their deployments in a way that the existing risk disciplines, individually, do not. It does not claim to be empirically validated at scale, exhaustive in its control coverage, or competent to deliver formal attestation absent qualified partnership. The work over the engagement period will determine which of these limits are dissolved by experience and which remain genuine constraints. The framework's discipline is to say what it is, what it is not, and how it will know which is which.
# Section 5: Illustrative Reference Application

## 5.1 Scope of the reference application

This section demonstrates the synthesis framework's application to a hypothetical reference deployment — a claims-processing agent built on LangGraph with public MCP servers, plausible as a Tier-2 European insurer configuration. No live engagement has been conducted at the time of writing. The demonstration's purpose is to make the methodology concrete enough to be evaluated; it does not, and cannot, substitute for the empirical validation the engagement period the broader project envisages will produce.

The reference deployment was selected for three reasons. First, claims processing is a financial-services use case under active development across European insurers as of late 2025 and early 2026, recognisable to second-line risk leaders and grounded in supervisory observation of insurer AI adoption — including EIOPA Opinion BoS-25-360's identification of claims management as a principal AI use-case in the insurance value chain, and DNB's 2024 reporting that 15 of 36 surveyed insurers had implemented AI in fraud detection and claim-amount estimation — rather than in speculative future configurations. Second, claims processing exhibits all five architectural properties identified in §1.4 and exposes all five threat patterns examined in §3.2 without requiring construction artificial to the use case. Third, the deployment context exhibits the financial-services amplifications documented in §3.3 — delegated transactional authority through claim approvals, concurrent data sensitivity regimes through health and personal data, supervisory scrutiny convergence through both the AI Act's Annex III treatment of insurance and DORA's ICT-resilience scope — by virtue of what insurance claims handling actually is.

The section is structured in four further sub-sections. §5.2 specifies the reference deployment's architecture, tool inventory, and principal model. §5.3 walks the synthesis framework through the deployment, threat enumeration first and control selection second. §5.4 presents one illustrative finding written in the form an actual engagement output would take. §5.5 closes with what the demonstration establishes and what it does not.

## 5.2 The reference deployment

The reference deployment is a claims-processing agent operating on behalf of a hypothetical Tier-2 European insurer. The insurer offers motor and home insurance to retail customers across two member states; claims volumes are in the low tens of thousands per month; the institution is subject to Solvency II oversight and falls within DORA's scope as a financial entity. The agent has been deployed in production for approximately six months at the point of the demonstration, supporting human claims adjusters by performing initial intake review, fact assembly, and recommendation drafting on incoming claims. Auto-approval authority is configured for claims under €2,500 with no fraud-risk indicators; claims above that threshold or with risk flags are routed to human adjusters with the agent's recommendation attached.

The agent's architecture is constructed in LangGraph, the LangChain Inc.-maintained open-source orchestration framework for building, managing, and deploying long-running, stateful agents (MIT-licensed; used in production by Klarna, Replit, Elastic, Uber, and J.P. Morgan among others). Foundation-model reasoning is supplied by a frontier model accessed via API, with prompt construction handled by the LangGraph orchestration layer. Tool access is implemented via the Model Context Protocol; the deployment connects to seven MCP servers, of which three are first-party (built and operated by the insurer's IT function), two are vendor-supplied (one for fraud-detection scoring, one for repair-shop estimate retrieval), and two are community-maintained MCP servers from the public ecosystem (one for a regional vehicle-data registry, one for general document parsing). Persistent memory is implemented via a vector store of past claim summaries that the agent retrieves at session initialisation; tool outputs are not persisted in memory across sessions by default.

The principal model is the source of the deployment's most significant assurance challenge. The agent operates with a single set of service credentials — a `claims_agent` service account with read access to the insurer's policy-administration system, the claims database, the customer-record system, the fraud-detection vendor's scoring API, and the document-management system. The same credentials are used for every claim the agent processes, regardless of which customer the claim concerns. Customer identity is communicated to the agent through the claim payload at session initialisation; the agent's reasoning is conditioned on the customer's identity, but the downstream tool calls do not carry the customer's identity through to the resource layer. The customer's claim handler — when the claim is routed to one — is identified at routing time and assigned via the claims-management system, but the agent's pre-routing actions are performed under the service account.

The deployment configuration is illustrative of patterns observable across European insurer AI deployments as of early 2026: agent-orchestrated workflows on top of established frameworks, tool access via MCP with mixed first-party and third-party servers, service-account principal models, and auto-approval thresholds calibrated to the institution's risk appetite. The configuration is deliberately calibrated to exhibit several risk surfaces — community-maintained MCP servers, a service-account principal model, an auto-approval threshold — because the demonstration's purpose is to show how the methodology responds to a configuration with non-trivial residual risk. A more conservative configuration would foreground fewer of the patterns the framework is constructed to address. The configuration should be read as a stress case representative of patterns observable in the market, not as a recommendation. The characterisation rests on inference from publicly-disclosed deployment patterns rather than on direct observation of any specific institution's configuration; the section's claim is that the configuration is plausible as a representative pattern, not that any specific institution operates exactly this configuration.

## 5.3 Methodology application

The synthesis framework is applied to the reference deployment in two sequenced passes: a *current-state* assessment that identifies the deployment's actual configuration, attack surface, and residual risk, and a *target-state* posture that specifies the controls and architecture the methodology recommends. Producing both passes is what differentiates the methodology from threat-model exercises that identify risks without specifying remediation, or from control catalogues that prescribe remediation without first establishing what is to be remediated.

### Current-state assessment

Current-state assessment begins with the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications as the structural reference and develops the cross-cutting patterns from §3.2 against the deployment's specific architecture. The framework's *framework-anchored* principle requires that findings be categorised legible to OWASP, AI Act, DORA, and ISO 42001 references rather than to internal taxonomies; the *evidence-first* principle requires that findings be paired with evidence requirements specifying what runtime data would be needed to detect or refute each finding in operation.

Applied to the reference deployment, the OWASP categories produce the following findings. ASI04 (Agentic Supply Chain Vulnerabilities) is present in elevated form because the deployment includes two vendor-supplied and two community-maintained MCP servers; the supply-chain attack surface is correspondingly broader than a deployment using only first-party servers. ASI03 (Identity & Privilege Abuse) is present in structural form because the service-account principal model permits the agent to access any customer's records regardless of the requesting principal's authority. ASI01 (Agent Goal Hijack) is present because incoming claim submissions are untrusted input the agent processes without the requesting customer's prior authorisation of any specific action. ASI02 (Tool Misuse) is present because the agent's tool inventory includes write-capable tools (claim-status updates, recommendation drafts) that could be invoked outside their intended scope. ASI06 (Memory & Context Poisoning) is present in latent form because the vector-store retrieval at session initialisation introduces past claim summaries — content the agent's earlier reasoning produced — into current reasoning context; corrupted historical entries propagate forward.

The five cross-cutting patterns from §3.2 are then enumerated against the deployment specifically. *Tool poisoning* is a present risk for each of the four non-first-party MCP servers; the two vendor-supplied servers are subject to vendor change-control discipline that mitigates but does not eliminate the risk, and the two community-maintained servers are subject to less stringent change control with corresponding higher residual risk. *Identity and privilege abuse* is the deployment's most significant structural risk: the gap between the service-account authority and any specific customer's authority is the entire population of policyholders the agent could in principle access. *Lethal trifecta* is present in the configuration: claim submissions are untrusted input, the policy-administration system contains sensitive data, and the recommendation routing produces an effective egress channel — though the egress is to internal systems rather than external destinations, which constrains exfiltration to insider-attacker scenarios rather than fully external ones. *Toxic flows* analysis identifies multiple paths from claim-submission ingestion through customer-data retrieval to recommendation routing, each of which warrants compositional review; the public MCP servers' integration into these paths increases the residual risk because compromise of the public servers could exploit the toxic flow without compromising any first-party component. *Zero-click exfiltration* is present in the form that the deployment processes claims asynchronously through a queue rather than synchronously per customer interaction; instructions embedded in claim submissions are processed without a customer present at the moment of processing.

The current-state assessment produces three artifacts. The first is a *threat register* mapping OWASP ASI categories and the five cross-cutting patterns to specific deployment components, with each finding annotated with its severity assessment, regulatory anchoring, and the evidence required to verify or refute the finding. The second is a *toxic-flow analysis* of the deployment's tool inventory using compositional-analysis tooling — the deployment's MCP architecture supports analysis with available tooling, though the LangGraph layer above the MCP integration requires bespoke flow modelling that current commercial tooling does not natively perform. The third is a *threat-model document* synthesising the register and the analysis into a single artifact suitable for presentation to the institution's second-line risk function and, where required, to supervisory engagement.

### Target-state posture

Target-state posture begins with the threat register and the gap analysis it implies, and selects controls from the synthesis framework's matrix that address the identified threats and close the identified gaps. The framework's *composable and continuous* principle requires that controls be specified for both initial deployment review and ongoing operation; the *principal-bound* principle requires that controls addressing identity and privilege abuse be foregrounded because of the deployment's service-account principal model; the *regulator-legible* principle requires that the control selection produce documentation supervisors can engage with using the regulatory vocabulary they themselves operate under.

The shape of the target-state posture is as follows: per-principal scope narrowing replacing the service-account model wherever architecturally feasible; an approved tool registry with cryptographic pinning replacing the current uncontrolled mix of community-maintained MCP servers; pre-deployment toxic-flow analysis as a gate on configuration changes; runtime policy gates on outbound actions and per-session evidence capture for after-the-fact reconstruction; and a consolidated assurance evidence repository structured for supervisory engagement.

Applied to the reference deployment, control selection from the matrix produces approximately fifteen primary controls and a larger set of supporting controls. The five primary controls examined illustratively in §4.3 are all selected for the deployment, with deployment-specific implementations:

TP-01 (tool metadata integrity verification) is implemented for all seven MCP servers, with stricter verification cadence for the two community-maintained servers given their elevated supply-chain risk profile.

IA-01 (principal propagation through tool chains) requires substantial architectural revision to the deployment's principal model. The framework recommends scope-narrowing controls in the absence of native principal propagation through the upstream systems; for the policy-administration system specifically, scope narrowing can be implemented via per-request entitlement checking that constrains the agent's effective authority to the customer whose claim is being processed. For the fraud-detection vendor's scoring API, the vendor's API does not support per-customer authorisation, and the framework's recommendation is to compensate via input-output validation at the agent layer rather than at the API layer.

LT-01 (lethal trifecta architectural review) produces an explicit accountability artifact; the deployment's lethal-trifecta configuration warrants explicit acceptance by the institution's CRO with documented compensating controls (claim-submission content filtering, recommendation review thresholds, and audit-log monitoring on a defined cadence).

TF-01 (toxic-flow analysis of authorised tool inventory) is implemented as a pre-deployment review and re-implemented on each material change to the tool inventory; the framework specifies quarterly cadence for re-analysis given the rate at which the public MCP server ecosystem evolves.

ZC-01 (asynchronous-processing risk gating) is implemented as runtime policy gates on the agent's recommendation routing, with claim-submission content scanning at ingestion and outbound-action policy checks before the agent issues a recommendation that would either auto-approve a claim below the €2,500 threshold or route a claim to a human adjuster.

In addition to the five primary controls, the matrix contributes ten further controls covering the deployment's specific risk surface: foundation-model version pinning and validation; vector-store integrity controls addressing the memory-poisoning risk; service-account credential rotation and just-in-time issuance where supported; logging architecture specifying the runtime-context capture required for after-the-fact reconstruction; and incident-response controls aligned with DORA's Article 17 (ICT-related incident management process) requirements covering detection, classification, response, recovery, and post-incident review.

The control-selection phase produces three artifacts. The first is a *control matrix extract* — the subset of the matrix's controls applied to the deployment, with deployment-specific implementation guidance for each. The second is a *control-gap memo* identifying the differences between the framework's recommended controls and the deployment's currently-implemented controls, with severity classifications for each gap. The third is a *remediation roadmap* sequencing the gap-closure work over an engagement period, with explicit acceptance pathways where the institution chooses to accept residual risk rather than implement a recommended control.

## 5.4 Output: a sample finding

The methodology's output is, in practice, an engagement report whose structure mirrors the assurance-engagement standards (ISAE 3000 / AT-C 205) the framework derives its evidence discipline from, while explicitly disclaiming the formal-attestation status those standards require. One illustrative finding from the reference deployment, written in the form an actual report would take, follows.

> **Finding IA-01-01: Service-account authority exceeds requesting-principal authority across customer record retrieval**
>
> *Severity: High. Regulatory anchors: AI Act Article 14 (human oversight); AI Act Article 15 (cybersecurity, robustness); DORA Article 9 (protection and prevention). OWASP ASI reference: ASI03 (Identity & Privilege Abuse).*
>
> The claims agent operates with a `claims_agent` service account holding read access to all policy-administration records, customer records, and claims data within the institution's systems. The agent processes claims for individual customers; for any given claim, the requesting customer's authority extends only to that customer's own records. The deployment does not propagate the requesting customer's identity through the tool chain; downstream resource access is authorised against the service account's authority rather than against the requesting customer's authority.
>
> Operationally, this configuration permits the agent to retrieve any customer's records when processing a specific customer's claim. The deployment's intended workflow constrains the agent's behaviour to retrieving only the requesting customer's records; the architectural authority to retrieve other customers' records is present and is exercised at the agent's discretion based on the model's reasoning over the runtime context. An attack pattern that manipulates the agent's reasoning context — for example, embedded instructions in a claim submission directing the agent to retrieve records associated with other customers and include them in the recommendation drafting context — exploits the gap between the agent's authority and the requesting customer's authority directly.
>
> The pattern is documented in production deployments outside this institution; the General Analysis Supabase/Cursor disclosure of July 2025 demonstrated the architectural vulnerability in a comparable configuration with synthetic data.
>
> *Evidence reviewed:* the deployment's identity-and-access-management documentation; sample tool-invocation logs covering 30 days of production operation; the policy-administration system's audit logs for the same period; the deployment's threat-model document.
>
> *Recommendation:* implement per-request scope narrowing at the policy-administration system, constraining the agent's effective authority to the customer whose claim is being processed. Where per-request scope narrowing is not architecturally feasible at the policy-administration layer, implement compensating controls at the agent layer that validate retrieved records against the requesting customer's identity before the records are incorporated into the agent's recommendation drafting context.
>
> *Residual risk after recommendation: Medium. Residual risk reflects that scope narrowing addresses the structural authority gap but does not address all variants of the threat pattern; specifically, attacks that manipulate the agent's reasoning to route the requesting customer's claim through tool chains that surface other customers' data via legitimate cross-reference operations would not be prevented by per-request scope narrowing alone.*

The finding's structure illustrates the methodology's outputs in operation. The finding is anchored in regulatory and standards references; the evidence reviewed is specified; the recommendation is operationally concrete; the residual-risk articulation acknowledges the recommendation's limits rather than treating implementation as full mitigation. The framing is supervisor-engageable: a supervisor reviewing the institution's documentation could trace the finding through to the underlying evidence and assess the institution's response on its own terms.

The full engagement output would include findings of comparable structure for each threat the methodology identified; the illustrative finding above is one of approximately fifteen primary findings the reference deployment would generate, plus a larger set of secondary findings covering operational hygiene, documentation gaps, and lower-severity issues.

## 5.5 What this reference application establishes and what it does not

The demonstration establishes three things and does not establish three others.

It establishes that the synthesis framework is operationally specifiable: the methodology can be applied to a specific deployment to produce specific threat enumerations, control selections, and findings expressible in the regulatory and standards vocabularies the framework anchors itself in. It establishes that the design principles produce coherent guidance under application, working together rather than producing contradictory recommendations. And it establishes that the framework's outputs are structured for supervisory and second-line engagement, addressing the *regulator-legible* principle in operation rather than only in design.

It does not establish that the framework's findings would be empirically accurate at deployment scale; the reference deployment is hypothetical, and the findings are the methodology's logical output rather than findings against an observed system. It does not establish that the controls are operationally feasible at the cost and integration complexity an institution would actually accept; the controls are plausible, but the trade-offs an institution would face — between control rigour and operational efficiency, between framework completeness and engagement scope — can only be characterised through live engagements. And it does not establish that the methodology's findings would scale across the diversity of agent deployment configurations institutions actually operate; the reference configuration is one pattern, and the methodology's adaptation to other configurations is part of the engagement work to come.

The demonstration is, in this sense, a sufficient proof of operational specifiability and an insufficient proof of empirical effectiveness. The framework's value cannot be definitively assessed from the demonstration alone; the assessment is the engagement work to come. What the demonstration does establish is that the framework is concrete enough to be evaluated, applied, and improved through that work — that the synthesis articulated in Section 4 is not abstract scaffolding but a methodology that produces specific outputs against specific deployments, in a form the institutions deploying agents and the supervisors examining those deployments can engage with.

# Section 6: The Regulatory Horizon

The synthesis framework Section 4 develops and Section 5 demonstrates does not exist in regulatory isolation. It exists within an architecture of regulation, supervisory guidance, and standards work that has accelerated through 2024 and 2025 and continues to evolve under partial political and institutional uncertainty. The argument of this section is twofold. First, that the regulatory architecture in force, in approach, and in active formation is sufficient — without further legislative action — to make assurance methodologies for agents in regulated financial services move from useful to operationally necessary within institutions' planning horizon. Second, that the synthesis framework occupies a specific position in this architecture: it does not propose new regulatory obligations; it provides operationally specified methodology for satisfying obligations that already apply or are imminent, with the regulator-legibility and framework-anchoring required for those obligations to be addressed in supervisory examination.

The section examines the architecture in four parts. §6.1 establishes DORA as the regulatory anchor in force. §6.2 addresses the AI Act and the timing uncertainty introduced by the Digital Omnibus proposal. §6.3 examines the convergent supervisory signals from national and European authorities through 2025. §6.4 frames the precedent — CBEST and TIBER-EU — that suggests how supervisory expectation around agent assurance is likely to develop, and locates the synthesis framework's position within this trajectory.

## 6.1 DORA as anchor

The Digital Operational Resilience Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2554) has applied to in-scope financial entities since 17 January 2025. DORA is, for the purposes of this paper's argument, the load-bearing piece of the regulatory architecture: it is in force, it covers AI-reliant systems within its ICT scope, it imposes obligations that agent deployments materially affect, and it provides the supervisory infrastructure within which agent assurance methodologies will be examined whether or not new agent-specific regulation is enacted.

DORA's core obligations relevant to agent deployments fall in four areas. Articles 5 through 16 establish the ICT risk management framework — governance, identification, protection, detection, response and recovery, learning, and reporting — including the simplified framework for entities qualifying under Article 16. The framework applies to all ICT systems supporting financial services, and AI-reliant systems are not exempt; agent deployments fall within scope as ICT systems supporting in-scope business functions. Articles 17 through 23 address ICT-related incident management, classification, and reporting, with obligations that apply to agent-related incidents on the same terms as other ICT incidents. Articles 24 through 27 establish the digital operational resilience testing regime, including threat-led penetration testing for significant entities. Articles 28 through 44 address ICT third-party risk management, with Articles 28 through 30 covering the general regime — third-party register, contractual requirements, exit strategies — and Articles 31 through 44 establishing the oversight framework for critical ICT third-party providers designated by the European Supervisory Authorities.

The third-party provisions warrant specific attention. Most agent deployments depend on third-party providers in multiple layers: foundation-model providers, MCP server operators, hosting and infrastructure providers, and integration vendors. DORA's third-party register requires institutions to identify and document these dependencies. The contractual requirements specify what arrangements with third parties must include — service definitions, data location, access rights, security standards, subcontracting limits, audit rights, exit provisions. The oversight regime brings the largest providers within direct supervisory engagement when designated as critical ICT third-party providers by the European Supervisory Authorities. The implication is that an institution's agent deployment is, under DORA, subject to substantively complete supervisory expectations on third-party risk management — including for foundation-model providers and MCP server operators meeting the relevant thresholds — without any additional regulation being required.

Two delegated regulations supply technical specification of the obligations most directly relevant to agent assurance. Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2024/1774 is the Regulatory Technical Standard on the harmonisation of ICT risk management tools, methods, processes, and policies. Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2025/1190, adopted 13 February 2025 and published in the Official Journal on 18 June 2025, is the Regulatory Technical Standard on threat-led penetration testing under DORA. A further RTS, Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2025/532 of 24 March 2025, addresses the elements financial entities must determine and assess when subcontracting ICT services supporting critical or important functions; while not directly an agent-assurance instrument, the subcontracting RTS is structurally relevant where agent deployments rely on subcontracted ICT chains. Together with DORA itself, the RTS supply the technical detail against which institutions' implementations will be examined.

DORA's relevance to agent assurance is therefore not contingent on AI-specific regulatory developments. An institution deploying an autonomous agent in a function within DORA's scope is already subject to obligations that the agent's architectural properties make difficult to satisfy without methodology specifically designed for the agent case. The synthesis framework's regulatory anchoring to DORA is not analogical; it is direct.

## 6.2 The AI Act and the Digital Omnibus question

The European Union Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) establishes a horizontal regime for AI systems, with obligations differentiated by risk classification. The Act's high-risk classifications under Annex III include credit scoring (Section 5(b)) and life and health insurance pricing (Section 5(c)), which together cover material portions of European insurer and bank business. The high-risk obligations under Articles 9 through 15 — risk management, data governance, technical documentation, record-keeping, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity — apply directly to systems classified as high-risk, and the obligations on providers (Article 16) and deployers (Article 26) determine which obligations attach to which actors in the value chain.

The Act's timing is the regulatory question most subject to political uncertainty as of the paper's writing. The Act entered into force 1 August 2024, with staggered application across categories: prohibited practices applicable from 2 February 2025, general-purpose AI obligations from 2 August 2025, and high-risk system obligations under Article 6(2) and Annex III from 2 August 2026 in the original text. The European Commission's Digital Omnibus on AI proposal of 19 November 2025 (COM(2025) 836) proposes to link the high-risk applicability date to the availability of harmonised standards and supporting Commission guidance, deferring stand-alone high-risk system obligations to 2 December 2027 at the latest and product-embedded high-risk system obligations to 2 August 2028. The second political trilogue between the European Parliament, the Council of the EU, and the European Commission on 28 April 2026 ended without agreement; a further trilogue was scheduled for 13 May 2026. The Council adopted its negotiating position on 13 March 2026; the Parliament's IMCO/LIBE committees published a joint report on the file (2025/0359(COD)) in early 2026.

The trilogue process remains in motion at the time of writing and the final timing is not yet settled. If the Omnibus is not adopted before 2 August 2026, the original AI Act timeline applies as written.

The argument of this paper does not depend on a single AI Act timing scenario. Two reasons. First, DORA applies regardless: the architectural properties of agents make their assurance difficult under DORA's obligations whether or not the AI Act's high-risk obligations apply on the original or the deferred timeline. Second, the AI Act's effect on regulated financial services operates through three channels — provider obligations on foundation-model providers, deployer obligations on the institutions, and standards-and-guidance obligations on the Commission and the European Standardisation Organisations — and only one of these (deployer obligations under Annex III) is directly affected by the timing question. Provider obligations on general-purpose AI models applied from 2 August 2025 and are not subject to the high-risk timing question. Standards work under the harmonised standards regime continues regardless of the deployer-obligation timing.

The implication for institutions deploying agents is that the regulatory architecture they must satisfy includes the AI Act in some form, on some timeline, with substantive obligations under either the original or the deferred date. Institutions waiting for timing certainty before establishing assurance methodology are waiting for a clarification that may arrive after the obligations apply, given that the standards work and supervisory examination practice take time to mature even after a regulatory date is fixed. The synthesis framework's anchoring to specific AI Act articles — Article 9 on risk management, Article 11 on technical documentation, Article 12 on record-keeping, Article 14 on human oversight, Article 15 on accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity — provides a regulatory referent that survives both timing scenarios.

## 6.3 Sectoral supervisory convergence

The most diagnostic signal as of early 2026 is not the trajectory of any single regulatory instrument but the convergence of supervisory communications from national and European authorities through 2024 and 2025. The convergence indicates that supervisors regard agent assurance as a current concern rather than a future one, and that the supervisory examination posture toward AI-reliant systems has materially evolved.

The German Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin) published *Guidance on ICT Risks in the Use of AI at Financial Entities* on 18 December 2025 (English version released 30 January 2026), a 35-page non-binding guidance document addressed primarily to CRR-regulated institutions and Solvency II insurers. The guidance treats AI systems as a sub-case of network and information systems under Article 3(2) DORA rather than as a separate regulatory category, and locates AI risk management within DORA's existing framework with reference to Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2024/1774 (the ICT risk management RTS) and Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2025/532 (the subcontracting RTS). The guidance specifies expectations on AI strategy approved by the management body, AI asset inventory and integration into the broader ICT asset register, ICT risk management throughout the AI lifecycle, third-party ICT risk for cloud-based and externally-supplied AI components, and technical countermeasures addressing data integrity, adversarial inputs, and cybersecurity vulnerabilities specific to AI. The document includes an illustrative case study of an institution operating an LLM-based AI assistant across different infrastructures, with analysis of risks under CRR and Solvency II. The guidance does not articulate agent-specific expectations, but its treatment of AI as ICT brings the paper's framework directly into BaFin's supervisory frame: agent deployments fall within ICT risk management, and the synthesis methodology produces evidence aligned with the BaFin-articulated expectations.

The French prudential supervisor ACPR has developed its supervisory approach to AI through a sequence of publications and speeches by First Deputy Governor Denis Beau, who is the Designated Chairman of the ACPR. The framing is built around four interdependent evaluative criteria — appropriate data management, performance, stability, and explainability — which ACPR has previously articulated as the cardinal points of a compass for AI in finance. Beau's October 2025 Lisbon address ("A compass to guide us toward intelligent AI supervision," 27 October 2025) reports that nearly all banks and insurance companies in France now operate AI systems and develops the supervisory methodology for AI under the AI Act. Beau's December 2025 article in *Archives de philosophie du droit* (Tome 66, November 2025; published on the ACPR site 12 December 2025) — *Implementing effective surveillance of AI in the financial sector* — addresses the legal, methodological, and operational challenges of AI surveillance in finance. ACPR's approach is notable for explicitly addressing cases that fall outside the AI Act's narrow high-risk scope, treating AI supervision as a matter of internal-control expectations under existing prudential regimes rather than as something coextensive with AI Act obligations alone.

The Dutch central bank DNB published *AI at Insurers: opportunities and risks* (Dutch: *AI bij verzekeraars: kansen en risico's*) on 27 March 2025, articulating supervisory expectations for AI deployments by Dutch insurers, based on findings from a 2024 industry survey, and building on the joint DNB-AFM SAFEST framework first introduced in 2019. The publication identified that 15 of 36 surveyed insurers had implemented AI in business processes including risk assessment, fraud detection, and claims processing automation, and DNB indicated that risk-based examinations of insurer AI implementations would follow in 2025. The Luxembourg supervisor CSSF, jointly with the Banque centrale du Luxembourg, published its *Second thematic review on the use of Artificial Intelligence in the Luxembourg financial sector* on 16 May 2025, surveying 461 institutions across investment firms, fund managers, credit institutions, e-money institutions, and payment institutions (86% response rate, with 402 use cases identified), documenting concrete AI use case adoption across regulated entities and articulating supervisory expectations.

The European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority issued *Opinion on AI Governance and Risk Management* (EIOPA-BoS-25-360) on 6 August 2025, addressed to competent authorities and covering the activities of insurance undertakings and intermediaries that use AI systems within the insurance value chain. The Opinion is grounded in the Insurance Distribution Directive (Articles 17, 20, 25), the Solvency II Directive (Articles 41, 46, 82), DORA (Articles 4, 5, 6, 11, 12), Commission Delegated Regulation 2015/35 (Articles 258, 260), and Commission Delegated Regulation 2017/2358 (Articles 6–9). It addresses baseline supervisory expectations across six areas: fairness and ethics; data governance; documentation and record keeping; transparency and explainability; human oversight; and accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity. It does not extend the AI Act's high-risk requirements to all AI systems used in insurance; rather, it interprets existing sectoral legislation in the AI context for AI systems that are neither prohibited nor classified as high-risk. The Opinion's principle-based structure is directly relevant to agent assurance: each of the six areas it specifies maps onto components of the synthesis framework's control matrix, and the agent-specific architectural properties identified in §1.4 require each area to be addressed in an agent-deployment-aware manner.

The European Banking Authority published a factsheet, *AI Act: implications for the EU banking and payments sector*, on 21 November 2025, accompanied by a detailed letter to the European Commission published 17 December 2025 setting out the outcome of an AI Act mapping exercise. The mapping focused on creditworthiness assessment and credit scoring, classified as high-risk AI under Annex III(5)(b) of the AI Act, and identified the relevant interactions between AI Act requirements on high-risk AI systems and EU banking and payments sectoral legislation — including the Capital Requirements Regulation (575/2013), the Capital Requirements Directive (2013/36/EU), the Consumer Credit Directive (2008/48/EC), the Mortgage Credit Directive (2014/17/EU), the Payment Services Directive ((EU) 2015/2366), DORA, and the EBA Guidelines on Internal Governance, Loan Origination and Monitoring, and PD/LGD estimation. The EBA's findings: that no significant contradictions exist between the AI Act and EU banking and payments legislation, that the AI Act is complementary to existing sectoral legislation, that DORA's framework substantially covers AI Act cybersecurity and business continuity requirements, and that the CRR and CRD already provide a comprehensive technology-neutral governance and risk management framework that can be applied to AI tools. No immediate new EBA guidelines are planned; instead the EBA committed to 2026–2027 activities supporting common supervisory approaches and providing input to the AI Office and the AI Board Subgroup on Financial Services.

The European Central Bank's Single Supervisory Mechanism, in its *Supervisory Priorities 2026–2028* published on 18 November 2025, identified ICT and operational resilience as Supervisory Priority 2, with explicit treatment of banks' use of AI — particularly generative AI — as a focus of supervisory engagement, including planned targeted horizontal workshops with selected banks on generative AI applications. The Bank of England Financial Policy Committee published *Financial Stability in Focus: AI in the financial system* on 9 April 2025, addressing the systemic risk implications of AI adoption in UK financial services across four areas: greater use of AI in banks' and insurers' core financial decision-making, greater use of AI in financial markets, operational risks in relation to AI service providers, and the changing external cyber threat environment. The Bank for International Settlements has published research through 2024 and 2025 addressing AI supply-chain concentration and macroprudential implications, including BIS Working Papers and the FSI Insights series on regulating AI in the financial sector.

Two observations follow from this enumeration. First, the publications collectively demonstrate that supervisory authorities across Europe regard AI deployments — including but not limited to autonomous agents — as a current concern requiring active supervisory engagement rather than a future concern awaiting regulatory mandate. The publications are non-binding in the formal sense, but supervisory expectations articulated at this volume and cadence shape examination practice; institutions whose AI deployments do not address them face examination risk under existing supervisory authority, regardless of any further legislative development.

Second, the publications converge on substantive themes despite differing institutional origins. AI deployments are treated as an ICT and operational-resilience matter under existing frameworks (BaFin, DNB, CSSF, ECB SSM); third-party concentration and supply chain are recurring concerns (BIS, BoE FPC); the gap between AI Act high-risk classification and the supervisory perimeter is acknowledged and addressed (ACPR specifically); governance, oversight, and adversarial-testing expectations recur across publications. The convergence suggests that institutions can, as of early 2026, anticipate the substantive content of supervisory engagement on agent deployments with reasonable confidence, even where formal obligations remain in development.

The synthesis framework's regulatory anchoring addresses this convergence directly. Where supervisory communications emphasise ICT risk management framing, the framework's controls anchor to DORA articles. Where they emphasise governance and lifecycle, the framework anchors to ISO 42001 and NIST AI RMF. Where they emphasise threat-led testing, the framework adopts the methodology directly. The framework does not require institutions to adopt new vocabularies; it organises existing vocabularies into the composition agent assurance requires.

## 6.4 The precedent and the position

The trajectory along which supervisory expectations around agent assurance are likely to develop is suggested by precedent. The most directly analogous precedent is the threat-led penetration testing regime: CBEST, established by the Bank of England in 2014 with the PRA, FCA, and FMID supervisory community; TIBER-EU, published by the European Central Bank in May 2018 as the European-level extension of the CBEST pattern; and the absorption of these patterns into DORA's threat-led penetration testing provisions in 2024 and 2025.

The CBEST/TIBER-EU pattern exhibited a specific developmental trajectory. The methodologies were initially supervisory innovations addressing threats (cyber-physical attacks on financial market infrastructure) the existing regulatory perimeter did not adequately address. They operated for several years as supervisor-led but non-mandatory regimes — institutions participated under supervisory invitation rather than legislative requirement. Through this period, methodology, intelligence-sharing, and examination practice matured. The eventual codification under DORA absorbed the matured methodology into binding regulation rather than constructing a regulatory regime de novo. The trajectory from supervisory innovation to legislative absorption took approximately a decade.

The application of this pattern to agent assurance is suggestive rather than predictive. The current state — supervisory communications converging on agent-related expectations, methodology emerging in the technical and standards communities, examination practice developing through engagement with institutions that have deployed AI — corresponds to the early phase of the CBEST trajectory. The question of whether supervisor-led methodology around agent assurance will eventually be codified into binding regulation, and on what timeline, cannot be answered from the precedent alone; the regulatory and political conditions differ. What the precedent does suggest is that the period during which methodology is established — through the engagement of supervisors, deployers, and assurance practitioners — is the period in which the expectations supervisors will eventually examine institutions against are formed.

The position the synthesis framework occupies in this architecture is therefore specific and bounded. The framework is offered as a contribution to the methodology development supervisory authorities are signalling will be required, anchored in the regulatory and standards stack institutions are already subject to. It does not propose new regulatory obligations; the obligations relevant to agent assurance arise from DORA, the AI Act, the sectoral supervisory expectations enumerated in §6.3, and the standards work continuing in parallel. It does not claim to be the methodology supervisors will eventually adopt; the development of supervisory examination practice for agents is the work of the supervisory authorities and standards bodies, and the paper's contribution is one input rather than a substitute.

The framework's value within this architecture is operational specification. Supervisory communications articulate expectations in terms appropriate to communication: governance, oversight, robustness, third-party risk management, adversarial testing. Institutions deploying agents need methodology that translates these expectations into specific controls, evidence artifacts, and test procedures. The synthesis framework provides such a translation, anchored in the regulatory and standards stack the supervisors themselves operate under, structured for the supervisor-legibility their examination practice requires. Whether the framework is adopted in the form Section 4 articulates, in some refined form following the engagement period to come, or as input to methodology developed by other contributors, the operational gap it addresses is not contingent on the framework specifically. The gap exists; some methodology will fill it; the framework is the paper's contribution to what that methodology should be.

The horizon, then, is not a deadline but a trajectory. The regulatory architecture in force makes assurance methodology for agents necessary as of early 2026 to satisfy obligations institutions are already subject to. The architecture in approach — the AI Act high-risk obligations on whichever timeline the trilogue resolves — adds further specification. The architecture in formation — the supervisory examination practice maturing through 2025 and 2026 — determines what supervisors will eventually expect to see; institutions whose methodology is in place when expectations crystallise will be in a different position from institutions whose methodology is still in development. The horizon is now, in the sense that the work of establishing assurance methodology is the work of this period, not of a future period when regulatory clarity arrives.

Section 7 turns to recommendations: the operational cadence at which institutions deploying agents in regulated financial services should be establishing the assurance posture this section's regulatory horizon requires.
# Section 7: Recommendations

The regulatory horizon Section 6 documented makes assurance methodology for agent deployments operationally necessary as of early 2026, and the synthesis framework Section 4 develops provides one structured response. This section translates the framework's principles into recommendations institutions deploying agents in regulated financial services can act on, organised by operational cadence: the next 30 days, 90 days, and 180 days. The cadences are illustrative rather than prescriptive; institutions with greater existing maturity can compress, while institutions earlier in their AI deployment trajectory may require longer. The structure reflects the order in which dependencies resolve — a 90-day action commonly depends on a 30-day artifact — and is intended to be useable by Heads of Model Risk, CROs, CISOs, and Heads of AI Governance reading the paper as the basis for their next planning cycle.

The recommendations rest on three premises that should be made explicit. First, that the institution either has agents deployed in production, has agents in active development for production deployment, or has reasonable expectation of acquiring agent capabilities through vendor offerings or internal development within the planning horizon; institutions for which agent deployment is purely speculative will find these recommendations premature. Second, that the institution's existing AI governance, model risk management, and ICT risk management functions are operating at a baseline level of maturity; recommendations assume these functions exist and can absorb agent-specific work, rather than presupposing their construction. Third, that the institution has not yet established assurance methodology specifically for autonomous agents; institutions with mature agent assurance practice are not the recommendations' primary audience.

## 7.1 The next 30 days

The 30-day horizon is for establishing the foundational artifacts on which subsequent assurance work depends. The actions are documentation-intensive rather than implementation-intensive; they produce inventory, classification, and accountability artifacts that 90-day and 180-day work draws on.

**Establish the agent inventory.** Identify every autonomous agent deployment currently operating in production, every agent deployment in active development for production, and every agent capability available through deployed vendor offerings. The inventory should record, for each deployment: the business function the agent supports; the foundation model or models the agent reasons over; the tools and integrations the agent invokes; the identity and authority model under which the agent operates; the principal model (service account, propagated identity, hybrid) governing downstream resource access; the volume and consequence surface of the actions the agent takes; and the human oversight regime applied to the agent's outputs. The inventory is the single most important 30-day artifact because every subsequent assurance action depends on knowing what is to be assured. Institutions discovering during inventory work that they cannot enumerate their agent deployments comprehensively — typically because business units have adopted vendor agent capabilities without central coordination — should treat the discovery itself as a finding warranting governance escalation.

**Establish regulatory mapping.** For each agent deployment in the inventory, identify the regulatory regimes the deployment falls within: AI Act high-risk classifications under Annex III where applicable; DORA scope determination; sectoral supervisory regimes (Solvency II, CRR/CRD, MiFID II, others as relevant to the institution's licences); GDPR special-category data treatment where applicable; and any sector-specific regulations the agent's function implicates. The mapping is not the same as a compliance assessment; it identifies which obligations apply, not yet whether they are satisfied. The mapping output is a document that, for each agent deployment, lists the applicable regulatory regimes with specific articles or sections cited, and is structured for use in supervisory engagement should the engagement be requested.

**Establish accountability.** For each agent deployment in the inventory, identify the accountable executive, the second-line risk owner, the third-line audit owner, and the technical owner responsible for operational maintenance. The accountabilities should be documented and acknowledged by the named individuals; ambiguous accountability is a finding warranting escalation. Institutions should also identify, at the institutional level rather than the deployment level, the executive accountable for agent assurance methodology overall — the role that owns the institutional response to the regulatory horizon Section 6 documented. In many institutions this will sit with the CRO or with a dedicated Chief AI Officer where one exists; in institutions without such a role, the accountability should be explicitly assigned rather than treated as a residual responsibility.

The 30-day artifacts collectively answer three questions a supervisor could in principle ask immediately: what agent deployments are operating in the institution; what regulatory obligations apply to them; and who is accountable for them. Institutions unable to answer these questions are in a structurally vulnerable position regardless of the technical controls they have implemented.

## 7.2 The next 90 days

The 90-day horizon moves from inventory to assessment. The actions in this period apply assurance methodology to the deployments identified in the 30-day inventory, identifying gaps between current configuration and the synthesis framework's recommendations, and producing the artifacts on which 180-day remediation work depends.

**Conduct threat modelling against the OWASP spine.** For each high-priority deployment identified in the inventory — typically the deployments with material consequence surface, such as those with delegated transactional authority or those processing high-sensitivity data — conduct threat modelling using the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications (2026 edition) as the structural reference and the five cross-cutting attack patterns from §3.2 as the analytical lens. The output is a threat register per deployment, mapping OWASP categories and the cross-cutting patterns to specific deployment components, with severity assessments and evidence requirements for each finding. The threat register's substantive content depends on the deployment; the register's structure should be common across deployments to support institutional aggregation and trend analysis.

**Conduct architectural gap analysis.** For each high-priority deployment, compare the deployment's current implementation against the synthesis framework's design principles: evidence-first (is runtime evidence captured, retained, and structured for assurance use?); framework-anchored (do the controls in place map to specific regulatory and standards articles?); regulator-legible (would the deployment's documentation be useable in supervisory engagement without translation?); composable and continuous (does the assurance approach accommodate configuration change, or does it certify a snapshot?); principal-bound (is identity and authority enforced at the interaction level, or aggregated in service accounts?). The gap analysis output is, for each deployment, a list of specific gaps with severity classifications and remediation pathways.

**Conduct toxic-flow analysis.** For each high-priority deployment, conduct compositional analysis of the authorised tool inventory using available tooling (Snyk Agent Scan TF001 or equivalent functionality where the institution's procurement and vendor-relationship policies permit; in-house implementation otherwise). The analysis identifies paths from untrusted-input nodes through privileged-data-access nodes to egress nodes, with each identified flow categorised as remediated, gated by compensating controls, or accepted as residual risk. The analysis is most consequential for deployments using third-party tools, vendor-supplied MCP servers, or community-maintained MCP servers, where compositional risk is elevated relative to fully first-party tool chains.

**Conduct adversarial testing.** For each high-priority deployment, conduct adversarial testing against the threat patterns the threat modelling identified. The testing should include indirect prompt injection scenarios (using benchmarks such as AgentDojo, the dynamic evaluation framework introduced by Debenedetti et al. at NeurIPS 2024 and maintained by ETH Zurich's SPY Lab with subsequent extension by the UK and US AI Safety Institutes), tool poisoning scenarios for non-first-party MCP servers, and zero-click exfiltration scenarios for deployments processing content asynchronously. Adversarial testing produces evidence either of the deployment's resilience or of specific exploitable vulnerabilities; in the latter case, the findings move directly into the 180-day remediation cadence.

The 90-day artifacts collectively constitute the institution's first comprehensive assurance assessment of its agent deployments. The assessment will, in most institutions, identify gaps the institution has not previously documented. The discovery should be expected rather than treated as a failure; the discovery is precisely what the assessment exists to produce, and the value of the assessment is in the structured surfacing of gaps that ad-hoc review would miss.

## 7.3 The next 180 days

The 180-day horizon moves from assessment to assurance posture. The actions in this period implement the controls the 90-day assessment identified as missing, establish the runtime infrastructure on which ongoing assurance depends, and produce the documentation that demonstrates the institution's assurance posture to supervisors and to itself.

**Implement priority controls.** For each high-priority gap identified in the 90-day analysis, implement the recommended control or a documented compensating control with explicit residual-risk acceptance. The implementation should be sequenced by severity, with critical and high-severity gaps prioritised. Implementation timelines for specific controls vary; the 180-day horizon does not assume every control is fully implemented within the period, but the implementation roadmap should be in place with milestones, owners, and explicit acceptance pathways for any gap the institution chooses to accept rather than remediate.

**Establish runtime defence and observation.** Implement the runtime infrastructure the framework's evidence-first principle requires: logging architecture capturing the runtime-constructed reasoning context to the granularity the framework's controls specify; monitoring infrastructure detecting anomalies in agent behaviour; runtime policy gates on outbound actions for deployments where zero-click risk warrants them; integration with the institution's existing security operations and incident response functions. The runtime infrastructure is the foundation on which continuous assurance depends; without it, assurance reduces to the point-in-time assessment the 90-day cadence already produced.

**Consolidate the supervisory-ready repository.** Establish a single repository — with appropriate access controls — containing, for each agent deployment: the inventory record; regulatory mapping; accountability documentation; threat register; gap analysis; toxic-flow analysis; adversarial testing results; implemented control documentation; residual-risk acceptance documentation; and runtime evidence supporting each control's operation. The structure should support supervisor-engageable retrieval — the institution should be able to produce, on supervisory request, the documentation relevant to any specific obligation under any specific regulatory regime, without translation work. The repository is the most consequential 180-day artifact: it is the artifact through which the institution demonstrates its assurance posture to itself, to its second and third lines of defence, and ultimately to supervisors.

**Establish ongoing cadence.** Specify the cadence at which each control's evidence is regenerated, the cadence at which the threat register is reviewed against new public disclosures, the cadence at which the toxic-flow analysis is re-run, the cadence at which the gap analysis is re-conducted, and the cadence at which the supervisory-ready repository is updated. The cadences should be documented in an institutional policy and acknowledged by the accountable executives; ad-hoc review is insufficient for compliance with the framework's composable-and-continuous principle.

The 180-day cadence does not produce a fully mature agent assurance posture; mature assurance is the work of years rather than months. What the 180-day cadence produces is a structured posture from which subsequent maturation can proceed: known deployments, classified obligations, assessed gaps, prioritised remediation, established runtime infrastructure, and a consolidated repository documenting all of the above. Institutions reaching this posture within 180 days are in a structurally different position from institutions still at the 30-day inventory stage, and the structural difference will be visible to supervisors examining the institution.

## 7.4 What these recommendations do not solve

The recommendations are operationally bounded. Three categories of work fall outside their scope and warrant explicit acknowledgement.

The recommendations do not address the question of formal attestation. The framework's outputs are assurance-ready evidence; formal attestation requires a qualified practitioner relationship the framework does not itself constitute, as Section 4 noted. Institutions seeking formal attestation should anticipate this work as a separate engagement track, sequenced after the 180-day cadence has produced the artifacts attestation would attest to. The decision to pursue formal attestation is institutionally specific; the recommendations above are structured to support either pathway.

The recommendations do not address foundation-model selection or vendor risk management for foundation-model providers specifically. The synthesis framework treats foundation-model behaviour as an input to assurance rather than as an object the framework directly assures; institutions face material decisions about which foundation models to use, under what commercial terms, and with what fallback arrangements, and these decisions interact with assurance methodology without being determined by it. The recommendations assume the institution makes these decisions through its existing vendor-risk and procurement functions, with input from the assurance methodology where the framework's outputs inform the decision.

The recommendations do not address the deeper question of whether specific agent deployments should exist at all. Institutions sometimes discover, through threat modelling and gap analysis, that an agent deployment carries residual risk the institution is not prepared to accept under any feasible compensating-control configuration. The synthesis framework's posture is that this discovery is itself a successful outcome of the assessment — the framework helps institutions distinguish deployments they should operate from deployments they should not — and the decision to retire, scope-narrow, or replace a deployment is institutional rather than methodological. The recommendations support the decision-making infrastructure; they do not substitute for the decisions themselves.

These limits are not deficiencies; they are the bounded scope appropriate to a methodology for assuring deployments that have been authorised to operate. The work the methodology does is the operational specification of how authorised deployments are assured; the work it does not do is the institutional governance of which deployments to authorise. Both are necessary, and the paper's contribution is to one of the two.

Section 8 closes the paper.
# Section 8: Closing

The paper has argued that the assurance of autonomous AI agents in regulated financial services is a structural problem rather than an incremental one, and that the structural problem can be addressed through a synthesis methodology that draws on the established disciplines of model risk management, AI governance, and cybersecurity without being subsumed by any of them. The argument has been developed across the seven preceding sections; this closing reflects on what the paper has done, what it has not done, and what comes next.

## 8.1 What the paper has done

The paper has made five connected claims, each of which Sections 1 through 7 develop in detail:

That autonomous agents in regulated financial services constitute a distinct assurance object, distinguished by five architectural properties — non-determinism, capacity for delegated authority, composite tool chains, runtime-constructed reasoning context, and semantic attack susceptibility — that the existing risk disciplines were not constructed to address in combination.

That model risk management, AI governance, and cybersecurity are necessary but insufficient for agent assurance — necessary because each contributes work the synthesis methodology presumes rather than replaces, insufficient because each addresses some subset of the architectural properties while leaving others outside its frame.

That five cross-cutting attack patterns — tool poisoning, identity and privilege abuse, the lethal trifecta, toxic flows, and zero-click exfiltration — recur across documented agent incidents and exercise the architectural properties in ways the existing disciplines do not natively address.

That an assurance methodology can be articulated through five design principles — evidence-first, framework-anchored, regulator-legible, composable and continuous, and principal-bound — that are jointly necessary and that, together, support a control matrix structured for supervisory and second-line engagement.

That the regulatory architecture in force, in approach, and in active formation is sufficient to make assurance methodology for agent deployments operationally necessary as of early 2026 for institutions with production or near-production agent deployments.

These claims are not novel in any single component — every element has antecedents in the literature, in supervisory practice, or in the technical security community's work — but in their composition into a methodology specifically for the assurance of agents in regulated financial services, anchored in the regulatory and standards stack the institutions deploying them already operate under.

## 8.2 What the paper has not done

The paper's contribution is bounded, and the boundaries warrant explicit acknowledgement.

It has not produced empirical validation of the methodology at scale. The framework has been developed against the public record of agent deployments, supervisory communications, and security disclosures, and applied to a hypothetical reference deployment. Empirical validation will accumulate over the engagement period the broader project envisages.

It has not delivered a turnkey methodology. The framework is preliminary, intended to be revised on the basis of what is learned. The control matrix is v1.0 of an ongoing artifact; specific controls will be refined as the methodology is applied to configurations not represented in the v1.0 set, particularly non-MCP architectures, hybrid deployments, and banking-sector-specific cases.

It has not constituted formal attestation, certification, or rating for agent deployments. The framework produces assurance-ready evidence; formal attestation requires partnership with qualified ISAE 3000 or AT-C 205 practitioners.

It has not addressed the question of whether specific agent deployments should exist at all. The framework helps institutions distinguish deployments they should operate from deployments they should not, but the decision to retire, scope-narrow, or replace a deployment is institutional rather than methodological.

These limits are not deficiencies; they are the bounded scope appropriate to a methodology offered into a developing field rather than to a settled discipline.

## 8.3 The audiences the paper addresses

To senior risk and compliance leaders in regulated financial services, the paper offers a framework with which to organise the assurance work that current and imminent regulatory expectations already require. The framework's regulatory anchoring is structured to be defensible in supervisory engagement; controls implemented under the methodology can be justified by reference to the regulatory regime the institution is already subject to rather than to vendor-specific best practice.

To supervisory staff at national and European authorities, the paper offers a vocabulary that aligns with the consensus taxonomy emerging through the OWASP GenAI Security Project and a methodology whose outputs are structured for direct supervisory engagement without translation. It is offered as a contribution to the methodology development that supervisory communications signal will be required, anchored in the regulatory architecture supervisors themselves operate under.

To the technical AI security community engaged with agent security, the paper offers a financial-services-specific application of the threat taxonomy the community has been developing. The contribution is not at the level of attack taxonomy — the paper adopts OWASP's categories and credits prior work explicitly — but at the level of the assurance methodology the taxonomy supports.

## 8.4 What comes next

Four streams of work follow directly. Empirical validation through engagement: the framework's application to live regulated deployments at scale is the work over which the methodology's claims about effectiveness can be evaluated. Methodology extension: non-MCP architectures, hybrid deployments, and banking-sector-specific configurations require the controls to be re-articulated at the appropriate abstraction layer. Engagement with supervisory and standards communities: participation in OWASP GenAI Security Project working groups, contribution to standards bodies' agent-specific work, response to supervisory consultations, and collaborative methodology development with academic researchers. And the development of formal attestation pathways through partnership with qualified practitioners and the institutional infrastructure formal assurance engagements require — a longer-term direction whose specific shape is not yet determined.

The streams are interrelated. Empirical validation generates the evidence on which methodology extension rests; methodology extension produces the artifacts that supervisory engagement requires; supervisory engagement informs the standards work that formal attestation pathways will eventually depend on.

## 8.5 The paper as one input

Agent assurance will not be established by declaring a new category of compliance. It will be established by producing evidence that supervisors, risk leaders, security teams, and deployers can all interrogate. This paper proposes one structure for producing that evidence. Its adequacy will be determined not by the elegance of the framework, but by whether it survives contact with real deployments, real incidents, and real supervisory examination. The work begins.
