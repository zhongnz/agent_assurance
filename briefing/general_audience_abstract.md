# General-Audience Abstract

In June 2025, security researchers showed how a crafted email could exfiltrate data from Microsoft 365 Copilot without the user clicking anything. Each component behaved as designed; the vulnerability lived in how the components composed. Composition is what autonomous AI agents do. As European banks and insurers move agents into production — for claims processing, fraud screening, customer interaction — they are encountering risks that the established disciplines (model risk management, AI governance, cybersecurity) were not constructed to address in combination. None covers the whole problem; none can be straightforwardly extended to do so. This paper argues that agent assurance in regulated financial services is a structural problem rather than a tooling problem, and proposes a methodology that draws on all three disciplines without being reducible to any. The methodology is articulated as five design principles, operationalised through a control matrix anchored to the AI Act, DORA, ISO/IEC 42001, NIST AI RMF, and OWASP's agent-specific threat taxonomy, and demonstrated against both a documented incident (the EchoLeak vulnerability) and a hypothetical reference deployment. The contribution is preliminary; the architectural argument is not.
