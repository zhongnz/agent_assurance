# Agent Assurance in Regulated Financial Services
*Briefing note on the position paper*

In June 2025, the public disclosure of CVE-2025-32711 showed that a single email could cause Microsoft 365 Copilot to leak sensitive data from a user's tenant to an external server. The user clicked nothing. The email-indexing system worked correctly. The retrieval system worked correctly. The model worked correctly. The vulnerability lived in how those components combined when an autonomous AI assistant acted across them. Banks and insurers in Europe are now moving similar systems into production: AI agents — software that interprets goals, reasons about how to pursue them, calls tools and external systems on its own initiative, and takes consequential actions — are being deployed for claims processing, fraud detection, customer service, internal operations, and increasingly for tasks that touch regulated activities. The deployment pace is outrunning the assurance frameworks the institutions rely on to govern technology and models.

Three disciplines govern technology risk in regulated financial services: model risk management, AI governance, and cybersecurity. Each was constructed for problems that agents resemble but do not fit. Model risk management presumes models whose behaviour can be reproduced from inputs at validation time; agent reasoning is probabilistic and depends on context the agent constructs at runtime. AI governance presumes management-system controls applied to a defined system; agents pull in content from many tools at once, including tools that may carry adversarial instructions. Cybersecurity — including the zero-trust architecture that succeeded the perimeter model — addresses attackers through identity, access, and per-action authorisation; agent attacks operate inside fully-authorised workflows, composing actions each of which an access-control system would individually permit. None of the three disciplines is wrong; each is necessary; none is sufficient on its own. The composition is the gap.

The paper argues that agent assurance in regulated financial services is a structural problem requiring a synthesis methodology — drawn from all three disciplines, anchored in the regulatory and standards stack the institutions already operate under: the AI Act, DORA, ISO/IEC 42001, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, and OWASP's *Top 10 for Agentic Applications*. The methodology is articulated as five design principles:

- **Evidence-first** — capture what the agent saw and did at the moment it acted, by design rather than as an afterthought.
- **Framework-anchored** — controls map to existing regulatory and standards obligations.
- **Regulator-legible** — assurance artefacts are usable in supervisory dialogue without translation.
- **Composable and continuous** — assurance addresses combinations of permitted actions, not individual actions.
- **Principal-bound** — the user on whose behalf the agent acts is the principal of record at every layer.

These principles are operationalised through a control framework, demonstrated against five recurring threat patterns observed in disclosed incidents, and applied to a hypothetical insurance claims-processing agent.

The contribution is preliminary. The framework is v1.0 of an artifact that will revise as it meets engagement reality. It does not constitute formal attestation, certification, or rating; it produces assurance-ready evidence that institutions and their auditors can use in their own work. The paper is addressed to second-line risk leaders in regulated financial-services institutions, security researchers and AI governance practitioners working on agent-specific problems, supervisors and regulators developing positions on agent assurance, and academic and policy researchers studying the trajectory. The full paper (~27,000 words, eight sections) develops the argument with primary-source citations. A populated control matrix (26 controls mapping all ten OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications categories, with ASI05 acknowledged as a coverage gap in v1.0; full regulatory anchoring), a retrospective case study against EchoLeak / CVE-2025-32711, a prospective reference application against a hypothetical insurance claims-processing agent, and a verification log documenting the audit trail for the position paper's 51 load-bearing claims are included alongside the paper.
